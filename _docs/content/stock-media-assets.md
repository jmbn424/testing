# Stock Media Assets

This compares the leading free and paid sources across the common kinds of stock media. These include photos, video, and illustrations, dedicated video footage, icons, fonts, audio, and motion graphics, plus other asset types such as 3D models, textures, and templates. Catalog sizes throughout are vendor-reported and should be read as approximate.

**\*Creative Commons and Public Domain Content restrictions**

Irrespective of license, if an image does not come with a model release (you should assume it doesn't by default unless it comes from a dedicated stock image library or you are explicitly assured by the copoyright holder that a model release was executed) then there cannot be any identifiable people. People can be in the image and clearly so, just as long as you canot determine who the person is from the photo alone, whether you know them or not.

1. No identifiable human faces or other identifiable information (name tag, etc.)
2. No brand logos
3. Attribution required (goes on [Credits](https://jrhealthins.org/credits) page)

Reserve for landscapes, skylines, objects, etc.

Images from the free catalogs above carry usage limits. For our marketing pages, skip any photo with an identifiable face or a visible brand logo: a free or Creative Commons license covers the photo, not the people or trademarks in it. Faces need a separate model release. See [Image licensing and usage rules](#image-licensing-and-usage-rules) below.

## Fonts

Fonts range from free open-source families to paid foundry releases, sold per style, as a one-time family license, or by subscription.

Font licenses depend on use, not on the file. The common tiers are desktop, webfont, app, and ePub or broadcast, and a desktop license alone does not cover putting a font on a website. Open-source families under the SIL Open Font License or Apache 2.0 can be self-hosted and shipped in products freely. Adobe Fonts is the main exception. It is bundled with Creative Cloud but cannot be self-hosted and stops working when the subscription lapses. A rental-to-own option also exists through [Fontstand](https://fontstand.com), where monthly rentals build toward owning the font.

### Free Font Catalogs

Most of these are open-source or libre libraries under the SIL Open Font License or Apache 2.0, which allow commercial use, self-hosting, and embedding with no visible attribution. The exceptions are large hobbyist portals like DaFont and 1001 Fonts, where many files are free for personal use only. License conditions are noted per row.

### Paid Audio Catalogs

The reason someone might pay for music is licensing certainty and reliable clearance. The dominant model is now a subscription that grants perpetual use of anything downloaded while active, with linked channels safelisted against YouTube Content ID claims; Pond5 still sells per-track for permanent use. Ownership is split three ways: Pond5 belongs to Shutterstock, Musicbed sits under the FM group, while Epidemic Sound, Artlist, and Soundstripe stay independent. AI-generated music is now a real presence inside several of these catalogs, so Content ID clearance is still worth checking.

Beyond the categories above, several other kinds of stock asset are licensed in similar ways. This section lists the main ones with their leading free and paid sources.

## Image licensing and usage rules

- **Every image has two locks, and you have to clear both.** Lock 1 is the photographer's copyright, which a Creative Commons or stock license opens. Lock 2 is the likeness and privacy rights of any person in the photo, which only a model release opens. A license never opens Lock 2.
- **Don't put identifiable people from Flickr or Wikimedia on marketing pages.** Free images (Flickr, Wikimedia, Unsplash, Pexels, Pixabay) almost never come with a model release. When you need faces, use paid, model-released stock (see the Paid Image Catalogs table above).
- **Landscapes, skylines, objects, and abstract images are safe.** No person, no Lock 2 problem.
- **Attribute when the license requires it** (Flickr and Wikimedia CC), using Title–Author–Source–License (TASL) on the Credits page.
- **Putting a ShareAlike image on a page does not make our whole site ShareAlike.** Only editing it into a new image triggers that. You can avoid the issue by using CC BY, CC0, or public-domain images whenever you plan to edit.

## Two things you must clear for every image

This is the core idea. Once it clicks, most decisions become obvious.

| Lock | What it protects | Who can unlock it | How we unlock it |
|---|---|---|---|
| **1. Copyright** | The photo as a creative work | The copyright holder (usually the photographer) | A valid license: CC BY, CC BY-SA, CC0, public domain, or a paid stock license |
| **2. Likeness / privacy** | The person depicted in the photo | The person in the photo | A signed model release |

A Creative Commons license only ever unlocks Lock 1. Creative Commons says so directly: their licenses "do not license… the publicity, personality, and privacy rights of third parties." So a valid CC photo of a stranger gives us the photographer's permission and no permission at all from the stranger.

## Quick rules by source

Cross-reference with the catalogs above. The "People OK?" column assumes marketing use on our site.

| Source | Attribution? | Editing? | People OK on marketing pages? | Watch out |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Unsplash / Pexels / Pixabay | No | Yes | No (no model release) | Don't resell or rebuild a stock library |
| Flickr (CC-licensed) | Yes (license-dependent) | Yes | No (no model release) | Verify the license. Mislabeling is common ("Flickr washing") |
| Wikimedia Commons | Usually yes (often CC BY-SA) | Yes | No (no model release) | Verify the license. Credit the author, not the uploader |
| Public-domain institutions (Met, Smithsonian, NASA, LoC, Rawpixel, Internet Archive) | No | Yes | No if a person is identifiable | Public domain clears Lock 1 only. People and trademarks stay uncleared |
| Paid stock with releases (Stocksy, Getty, iStock, Adobe Stock, Shutterstock, Alamy) | No (creative/RF) | Yes | Yes (releases included) | The right tool whenever we need real faces |

**Bottom line:** free sources work well for places, things, and abstract imagery. The moment you need an identifiable person, switch to paid, model-released stock.

## Which licenses we're allowed to use

Our rule: free, commercial use allowed, and modification allowed. These licenses meet it:

| License | Commercial use | Editing | Attribution | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **CC BY** | Yes | Yes | Required | Simplest CC license. Best choice if you plan to edit. |
| **CC BY-SA** | Yes | Yes | Required | ShareAlike. Matters only if you edit it into a new image (see If you edit an image). |
| **CC0** | Yes | Yes | Courtesy only | Creator waived all rights. |
| **Public Domain** (verified) | Yes | Yes | Courtesy only | Verify the file really is PD. |

Never use anything marked NC (NonCommercial, e.g., CC BY-NC) or ND (NoDerivatives, e.g., CC BY-ND). Both fail our criteria.

**Two kinds of "public domain," and the difference matters:**
- CC0 is a real legal waiver: the rightsholder actively gave up their rights, so it's reliable.
- The Public Domain Mark (PDM) is just a label anyone can put on a file they think is free. Treat PDM with more caution and spot-check it.

**License version matters, and Flickr and Wikimedia files vary:**
- CC 4.0 (the current default) gives a 30-day grace period: if you forget attribution and fix it within 30 days of noticing, your rights are restored. CC 2.0 and 3.0 have no grace period, so a slip terminates the license until the creator reinstates it. Attribute carefully, especially on older files.
- In 4.0, including the title is optional, though still good practice. In older versions it's required.

## How to attribute (TASL)

Put attributions on the Credits page. Creative Commons recommends four elements: Title, Author, Source, and License, each linked where possible.

| Element | What it is | Link it to |
|---|---|---|
| **T**itle | The work's title (if given) | the source page |
| **A**uthor | The name the creator chose (real name, username, pseudonym) | the author's profile |
| **S**ource | Where the image lives | the original photo/file page |
| **L**icense | The specific license and version | the license deed |

**Copy-paste template** (modified image; for an unmodified image, drop the change note):
```
This image is a cropped, color-adjusted version of "Golden Gate at Dusk"
by Jane Doe, used under CC BY 4.0. Modifications by [Company].
```

## If you edit an image

Two things to remember.

**1. Always note that you changed it.** Every CC license requires you to indicate modifications. Add a short note to the Credits entry: "Modifications: cropped; color-adjusted; text overlay added."

**2. ShareAlike only attaches to adaptations.** Displaying a CC BY-SA image, even beside our marketing copy, is a collection, not an adaptation, so it never makes the page or site ShareAlike. This was confirmed in *Drauglis v. Kappa Map Group* (2015), where a CC BY-SA photo on a commercial atlas cover left the atlas a collective work, not a derivative. Only editing the image into a new work triggers ShareAlike, and then *that new image*, not our site, must be CC BY-SA. Resize, format conversion, and trivial crops are fine; heavy recolor/filter, compositing, or large text/graphic overlays are adaptations.

**The easy way to avoid all of this:** if you plan to meaningfully edit an image, pick a CC BY, CC0, or public-domain source. Reserve CC BY-SA for images you'll use unmodified, or only resize or trivially crop.

## People in photos: the rule that keeps us out of court

Free CC and stock licenses unlock the copyright, never the person's likeness. For our commercial health-insurance pages, that gap is the real exposure.

**What can go wrong:**
- **Right of publicity / misappropriation.** Using an identifiable person's image to advertise without consent is actionable. It's state law and varies, but strong statutes (e.g., California Civil Code § 3344, New York Civil Rights Law §§ 50–51) allow statutory damages, the violator's profits, and attorney's fees. California's 2025 amendment also added injunctions and AI "digital replica" coverage.
- **False endorsement:** implying the person is a client of the brokerage or endorses it.
- **False light and defamation:** implying an ordinary person is sick, a patient, or insured by us. The health context makes this acute.

**The cautionary tale, Chang v. Virgin Mobile.** A teenager's photo was posted to Flickr under a valid CC BY license. An advertiser used it on billboards with proper photographer credit but no model release. The family sued for misappropriation. The photographer's license was fine, but it couldn't give away the teen's likeness. The case ended on a jurisdictional technicality, not because the use was acceptable.

**What to do:**
1. **Default:** don't use identifiable people from free sources on marketing pages.
2. **Safe substitutes:** people who aren't identifiable (faces hidden, backs of heads, someone shown only from behind such as a person holding a pet, crowds where no one is singled out), or no people at all.
3. **When you genuinely need faces:** use paid, model-released stock (see the Paid Image Catalogs table above). The premium you pay there mostly buys these releases and legal indemnification.

"Identifiable" isn't only the face. A distinctive tattoo, clothing, or context can make someone recognizable.

## Buildings and skylines

Low risk, but not zero:
- **Wide, generic skylines and landscapes:** fine, no release needed.
- **A single recognizable private building as the focus,** or prominent brand logos or signage: avoid these, or use a wider shot. (Edge cases can carry restrictions, for example a landmark with trademark or lit-display rights.)
- **Rule of thumb:** if no single private building or logo is the subject, you're fine.

## Disclaimers and disclosures (beyond attribution)

- **Modification note:** required on the Credits entry whenever you edit a CC image.
- **No endorsement:** never arrange an image or credit to suggest that the photographer or any person shown endorses the brokerage or is a client of it.
- **A "models are illustrative" disclaimer is not a fix.** It may improve the optics, but it doesn't supply a missing model release or clear right-of-publicity exposure. The reliable control is simple: no identifiable people without a release.

## Before you publish: checklist

- [ ] License allows commercial use (no NC) and editing (no ND). Record the license and version.
- [ ] Uploader plausibly owns it (consistent style and quality, clean reverse-image search). Watch for "Flickr washing."
- [ ] Saved a dated screenshot or PDF of the license page, and recorded the TASL info.
- [ ] Any identifiable person? If so, stop and use released stock or non-identifiable imagery.
- [ ] A recognizable private building or logo as the subject? Use a wider, generic shot.
- [ ] Editing it? If it's CC BY-SA, keep edits trivial or switch to CC BY, CC0, or PD, and add the modification note.
- [ ] Credits-page entry drafted (TASL plus modification note).
