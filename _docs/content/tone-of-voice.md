# J&R Health Insurance: Tone of Voice Guide

From the owners:

> Our clients' technical aptitude varies greatly. While some are quite comfortable navigating online processes, a good portion of our senior and Medicare clients require hands-on guidance. In my own experience, I've had multiple clients (not yet Medicare age but approaching it) who had email addresses but preferred we communicate exclusively via calls and texts. This could suggest a general preference for direct, voice-to-voice or SMS support rather than email correspondence.
>
> Since health insurance can be a complex topic, we want our clients to feel supported, confident, respected, and calm when they visit the site. The website should be straightforward, easy to navigate, and clearly outline our services. As much as possible, we would like to avoid pushy sales tactics or overly abstract/emotional language seen on other websites I've reviewed in adjacent industries, given that most clients seek clear and practical information.
>
> I'd also love for our website to reflect our core values of honesty and integrity. When clients visit the site, I want them to immediately feel that we are trustworthy.

## Why this guide exists

People come to a Medicare broker's website during one of the more confusing financial decisions of their lives. They're often anxious, often older, often helping a parent. The way we write should make that easier, not harder.

This guide defines how J&R Health Insurance sounds across our website, emails, and any client-facing copy. It exists to keep our voice consistent whether the writer is Rowena, Jasmine, an outside contractor, or a tool helping draft a first pass.

## Who we're writing for

Our primary readers are adults aged 65 and over approaching or navigating Medicare enrollment, including veterans; their adult children, who often do the research on a parent's behalf; and self-employed individuals and families looking at ACA plans.

Most readers are not insurance professionals. Some are reading about Medicare for the first time. Many have been worn down by aggressive marketing from national carriers or call-center brokers. We are writing to people who want a calm explanation from someone they can trust.

When readers finish a page on the site, they should feel supported, confident, respected, and calm. Those four words are a useful gut check before publishing: if a draft would leave the reader feeling rushed, talked down to, or sold to, it needs another pass.

## Voice attributes

Our voice is built on four traits. They work together; no single one should dominate.

### 1. Warm

We sound like a person, not a corporation. We use "we" and "you." We acknowledge that this stuff is stressful. Small bits of personality are welcome (a phrase like "Insurance Jungle," borrowed from a client review) without crossing into cutesy or unprofessional.

Warm is not the same as emotional or abstract. We avoid the inspirational register common in adjacent industry marketing (no "your healthcare journey," no "peace of mind awaits"). Concrete is warmer than abstract: "we'll walk you through it" reads warmer than "we're here for you every step of the way."

### 2. Plain-spoken

We translate. "Premium" becomes "the monthly bill." "Out-of-pocket maximum" becomes "the most you'll pay in a year." When industry terms are unavoidable, we define them in passing the first time they appear. We never assume the reader knows the difference between Medicare Advantage and Medigap until we've explained it.

### 3. Honest

We educate first and sell second. We mention that brokers are paid by carriers rather than clients, because that fact builds trust and answers the "what's the catch" question before it's asked. We acknowledge tradeoffs (no plan is perfect for everyone). We never use scarcity, manufactured urgency, or fear to drive action.

### 4. Steady

Medicare deadlines are real, and we name them when relevant, but we frame timing as information, not alarm. We don't catastrophize. We project the calm competence of a broker who has done this thousands of times and knows it will work out.

## What we are not

It helps to name what we're avoiding:

- **Not corporate.** No "We strive to deliver innovative solutions."
- **Not salesy.** No "Call now to lock in your savings!"
- **Not patronizing.** Older readers are intelligent adults; we don't talk down.
- **Not jargon-heavy.** Industry terms are translated, not assumed.
- **Not urgent or fear-based.** We don't manufacture anxiety to drive enrollment.
- **Not generic.** We sound like a small family agency, because we are one.

## Mechanics

A handful of editorial decisions apply sitewide.

- **No em dashes.** Use colons, parentheses, semicolons, or split the sentence. Em dashes read as an AI tell in web copy.
- **Bullets only when the content is genuinely list-shaped.** Prefer flowing prose. A description of how we work belongs in paragraphs; a list of plan types belongs in bullets.
- **Sentences on the shorter side.** Aim for an average around 15 to 20 words. The occasional long sentence is fine for rhythm; a steady diet of them is fatiguing.
- **Active voice by default.** "We compare plans" beats "plans are compared by us."
- **Contractions are welcome.** "We'll," "you're," and "don't" make us sound human.
- **Numbers in body copy.** Write out one through nine; use numerals for 10 and above. Exceptions: ages ("a 65-year-old"), dates, and dollar amounts always use numerals.
- **Compliance disclaimers** ("We do not offer every plan available..." and similar) live in the footer, not in body copy.
- **Calls and texts before email or forms.** Many clients prefer voice or SMS, even when they have email on file. When the copy invites contact, lead with phone and text; email and web forms are secondary options, not primary CTAs.

## Do and don't, in pairs

| Don't | Do |
|---|---|
| Maximize your healthcare ROI with our comprehensive Medicare solutions. | We help you compare Medicare plans and pick the one that fits your life. |
| Our team of expert insurance professionals is standing by. | When you call, you'll speak directly with Rowena or Jasmine, not a call center. |
| Don't miss the Open Enrollment deadline! | Open Enrollment runs October 15 through December 7. We'll walk you through it when you're ready. |
| Unlock unbeatable savings on premium coverage. | There's no fee to work with us, and most clients save money on their plans. |
| We leverage industry-leading partnerships with top carriers. | We work with every major carrier, so we can compare options honestly. |
| Schedule a consultation with one of our licensed agents today. | Give us a call. We'll spend as much time on the phone as you need. |
| Our proprietary process ensures optimal plan selection. | We sit down with you, listen, and walk through the options together. |

## How voice shifts by context

The four traits stay constant, but emphasis shifts.

**Marketing pages** (home, about, services): Warmth and honesty lead. We are earning trust.

**Educational content** (blog posts, FAQs, explainers): Plain-spokenness leads. The job is to make something complicated feel manageable.

**Transactional copy** (forms, confirmations, error messages): Steady leads. Reassure, give clear next steps, and don't drown the reader in personality.

**Compliance and legal text** (footer, privacy policy): Plain-spoken to the extent the law allows. The content is constrained, but we can still avoid making it more impenetrable than necessary.

**Emails and direct outreach**: All four traits apply, but warmth ticks up. These are addressed to a specific person, often by name. A short opening line acknowledging context ("Good to hear from you, hope your move is going well") is welcome.

## A few words about our audience

Two specific notes about writing for our readers.

**On age.** Most of our clients are over 65. They are not technologically helpless, they are not confused, and they are not in a hurry. They will read the whole sentence. They appreciate it when we write clearly and skip the hard sell. Avoid "seniors" as a noun when possible; "people on Medicare" or "people 65 and over" wears better.

**On veterans.** Veterans navigating Medicare often have additional coverage through the VA, and the interaction between the two is a real source of confusion. When writing for veterans, lead with respect for what they already know, not assumptions about what they don't.

## A final check

Read drafts aloud before publishing. Sentences that sound stilted or overly formal when spoken usually read the same way on screen. Revise until the copy sounds like a natural conversation with a client.
