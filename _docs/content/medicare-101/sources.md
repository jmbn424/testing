# Sources for Medicare 101 content generation by Claude, Gemini, and Perplexity

**Last updated:** May 27, 2026

This document records the source material consulted while researching and verifying the Medicare 101 page (`medicare-101-page.md`). It exists to make the page auditable: every factual claim on the page can be traced back to one or more sources below. It is one of three supporting documents for the page, alongside the process and methodology document ([medicare-101-content-generation-process.md](medicare-101-content-generation-process.md), which records how the page was built) and the maintenance document ([medicare-101-content-maintenance.md](medicare-101-content-maintenance.md), which tracks when content needs re-verification). Three independent research passes were conducted (Claude across three rounds of deep research, plus accuracy audits by Gemini and Perplexity), and all three signed off on the final draft.

**Provenance.** Claude assembled this document by reconciling URLs inferred from its own research work with citation lists Gemini and Perplexity produced directly when asked to enumerate their sources. All URLs were subsequently checked for liveness in a separate verification pass.

**How to read the list.** The factual citation tiers are organized into four sections: (1) **Shared sources**, cited by two or more models; (2) **Claude's unique sources**; (3) **Gemini's unique sources**; and (4) **Perplexity's unique sources**. Within each section, entries are grouped by publisher and listed by title and URL. Primary sources (CMS, state statutes, official agency publications) carry the most weight; secondary sources (broker sites, advocacy groups, news outlets) were used for context and corroboration. Where two sources conflicted, primary sources were treated as authoritative.

## Notes on methodology and source quality

This three-model cross-verification protocol surfaced several errors that single-source research would have missed, including the Oregon SHIBA agency-attribution correction (DCBS → ODHS), the California Medi-Cal asset limit reinstatement, the death of California SB 242 in February 2026, and the death of Tennessee SB 2575 / HB 1848 at the 114th General Assembly's sine die adjournment on April 23, 2026. It also surfaced and rejected several model hallucinations, most notably Gemini's proposed pending-legislation tracker, which paired real bill numbers (WA SB 5032, WA HB 1201, GA SB 401) with fabricated Medicare-related subject matter, and Gemini's claim of a "91-day" California birthday-rule window (the statute actually provides at least 30 days, with most carriers voluntarily extending to 60). For the phase-by-phase account of how each error was caught and corrected, see [medicare-101-content-generation-process.md](medicare-101-content-generation-process.md).

**A note on broker sites and the California birthday rule.** Multiple broker websites in Perplexity's secondary sources (MedicareNationwide, several others) describe the California birthday rule as "a 90-day window starting 30 days before the birthday and ending 60 days after," and some cite Insurance Code Section 10192.62. This framing is widespread but incorrect on both counts: the operative statute is California Insurance Code § 10192.11(h)(1), which guarantees an annual open enrollment period of "30 days or more, commencing with the individual's birthday." The "30 days before" element is the insurer's notice obligation, not the consumer's enrollment window. Most carriers voluntarily extend the consumer window to 60 days, but it begins on the birthday, not before. The page's current language ("a 60-day window each year, starting on your birthday") follows California Health Advocates' consumer-facing description; the parenthetical link to CHA's specific birthday-rule article is intended to give readers the most current authoritative detail.

**Source priority used when claims conflicted.** Primary sources (CMS fact sheets, statute text, state legislature trackers, official agency publications) were treated as authoritative. Secondary sources (broker sites, advocacy groups, news outlets, AI summaries) were used for context and corroboration only. Where two sources conflicted, primary sources won.

For ongoing maintenance triggers (when these sources need to be re-checked), see [medicare-101-content-maintenance.md](medicare-101-content-maintenance.md).

## Inspiration sources (structural and editorial reference, not factual citation)

The following pages were user-provided at the outset and used as inspiration for structure, topic coverage, framing, and voice. They are listed here for transparency about what was looked at during scoping, but they are deliberately separated from the factual citation tiers above. No content was lifted from any of these pages, and no factual claim on `medicare-101-page.md` is sourced from this list. Where these pages stated facts that ended up on the page, those facts were independently verified against primary sources (CMS, statute text, official agency publications) and are cited from those primary sources in the tiers above.

The original project scope included an explicit ethical commitment: use examples for inspiration without lifting anything directly. That commitment governed the work from the first draft onward. Common structural patterns visible across the industry (how Parts A through D are typically introduced, the Original-Medicare-vs-Medicare-Advantage framing, the inclusion of an FAQ block) were absorbed as conventions of the genre; the page's wording, section organization, examples, and editorial voice were composed independently. After the first draft was complete, a check against the inspiration set confirmed sufficient differentiation. The Phase 2 state-specific accordion content (added later for Washington, Oregon, California, Georgia, Tennessee, and an "All Other States" catch-all) differentiates the page further, since no single inspiration source covers the five-state mix or the specific Medigap, SHIP, Medicaid, and regulator details those blocks provide.
