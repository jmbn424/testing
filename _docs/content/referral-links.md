# Referral links on external sites

Example blurbs and a short usage guide for partners who offer to link to us.

Use this when someone organically offers to link to us from their site. The support is welcome, and a link like that can be a strong SEO signal, so it's worth making the process painless for the referrer.

These are example referral blurbs a partner site can use to place a link to [J&R Health Insurance](https://jrhealthins.org/). Each is written to read like a genuine recommendation, carry SEO value through its surrounding keywords, and stay on the cautious side of CMS Medicare marketing rules. Compliance-sensitive wording is flagged in the compliance note so a qualified professional can review it.

## How these blurbs are being offered

These are suggestions, shared with people already in J&R's network who offered, on their own, to link back because they value the work J&R does. The blurbs exist to make that easy, not to script it. We encourage each partner to put the recommendation in their own words: it keeps the endorsement genuinely theirs, and it mirrors how real recommendations actually read, since no two people describe the same thing the same way. Copying a blurb as-is is completely fine too. The choice, like the wording, stays in their hands.

The arrangement has a few defining traits. It's organic and unpaid: no fee, no compensation, no required reciprocal link, no quid pro quo, and the links are offered freely on the merits. The recommendation is editorial and belongs to the partner, with J&R providing only optional language they can use, cherry-pick from, or ignore. And it isn't a paid-sponsorship channel. As of this writing, the mechanism isn't meant to extend to paid placements. If J&R ever pursues paid sponsorships, those would run through a separate process (disclosed, tagged `rel="sponsored"`, and reviewed against the rules that apply there) rather than through these suggested blurbs.

This matters because the unpaid, editorial nature is what lets these links count as genuine endorsements (dofollow under Google's guidelines) and keeps the linking partners outside CMS's definition of a *compensated* lead generator. Keeping the two channels separate, organic suggestions here and any future paid work on its own track, protects both.

---

## Why there are multiple blurbs

The blurbs aren't interchangeable copies of one block. Each is worded differently on purpose, so partners use a *different* blurb (or adapt one into their own voice) instead of everyone pasting the same text. There are a few reasons for that.

The first is footprint. Search engines can spot the exact same passage running verbatim across many sites, and that pattern reads as a coordinated campaign rather than a set of independent endorsements, which can get the links discounted even when they're genuinely organic, as these are. Identical text is also duplicate content: with the same block on multiple pages, search engines tend to credit one version and discount the rest, so each link passes less value than distinct wording would. The last reason is simpler. Real recommendations from different people come out phrased differently, so varied blurbs make the link profile look as authentic as it is.

## How to use these

- Encourage each partner to put the recommendation in their own words. If a partner would rather use the supplied text, give different partners different blurbs so the same block isn't repeated across sites. The reasons are in the "Why there are multiple blurbs" section.
- Keep the links dofollow. These are editorial recommendations partners offered freely, with no payment or quid pro quo, so they can pass full link value. A normally inserted link is already dofollow, with nothing to switch on. The one thing to avoid is `rel="nofollow"`, which some site platforms and SEO plugins add to outbound links automatically. If a placement ever becomes paid or exchanged, switch that one to `rel="sponsored"`.
- Localize where it fits. For a partner with a local audience, "Washington State" can be swapped for any area where J&R serves, at any granularity: "Oregon", "Georgia", "Puget Sound", "Pacific Northwest", "Puyallup, WA", and so on. Don't invent coverage areas.
- Keep the company name in the anchor. The recommendation and the surrounding keywords can be reworded freely, but the linked text should stay "J&R Health Insurance" and the URL should stay "https://jrhealthins.org/". For example:

```html
  <a href="https://jrhealthins.org/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">J&amp;R Health Insurance</a>
```

  That branded anchor, the link sitting on the company name (**J&R Health Insurance**), is the safest and most natural pattern. An exact-match keyword anchor is the loudest single signal of manufactured link building, since real links rarely use the precise phrase a site is trying to rank for. The risk is higher here, with links going up around the same time, from a cluster of related contacts, using language J&R supplied. Keeping the clickable words on the brand name avoids stacking an anchor footprint on top of that timing-and-source pattern, and keeps the whole effort reading as what it is: a set of independent recommendations.

## Why these should perform for SEO

The link sits on the company name while the surrounding sentence carries the terms Google reads for relevance: Medicare, Washington State, turning 65, veterans, licensed broker or agent, and enroll. That's the natural-link pattern search engines reward, rather than the exact-match anchor they penalize. "Washington State" is a high-value term for a regional service, and localizing to a tighter area sharpens it further. The blurbs also read like something a real person would write, which matters under recent helpful-content and spam updates: a stuffed paragraph can be demoted, so they stop short of cramming. And the varied openings (statement, question, veteran-led, problem-framing) keep partners from all publishing the same block of text.

## Compliance note

The blurbs are written conservatively and already avoid the usual CMS triggers: no superlatives like "best," no specific plan benefits, premiums, or dollar figures, no "5-star" claims, and nothing implying government endorsement. Little review should be needed.

Even so, it's worth checking with Advocate Health Advisors FMO on whether a TPMO disclaimer needs to appear on a partner's page. It's unlikely to apply here, since the links are organic and partners aren't paid to generate leads, but it's a worthwhile one-time confirmation.
