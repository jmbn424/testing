**4. Gravitas-forward (for higher-stakes or formal instruments)**

## § 00 — How to use this guide

This is the standard for the voice, language, and structure of every legal document we publish for a general audience. It exists so that a writer, designer, or lawyer can make the same call we would, without guessing.

Our readers are a mix of everyday users and other professionals. We write for both at once by writing plainly: clear text serves the non-expert and wastes no one's time. Deliberately dense "legalese" is the opposite of what we do — it is a waste of the reader's effort and sends the wrong signal about who we are.

**What this guide is**

- A reusable standard you apply to any contract or disclosure, in any industry.
- A set of defaults — United States, with California compliance built in — plus optional layers you switch on for a specific industry or region (see § 14).
- A practical tool: rules, word lists, do/don't examples, and a checklist.

**What this guide is not**

- It is not legal advice and does not replace a supervising attorney's judgment.
- It does not override any law, regulator requirement, or mandated form wording. Where the law dictates exact text, the law wins (see the precision exception in § 17).
- It is not a brand or marketing voice guide; it governs legal and regulatory content specifically.

> **The order of operations**
> When two instructions seem to conflict, resolve them in this order: **(1)** the law and any mandated wording, **(2)** the supervising attorney's documented decision, **(3)** this guide, **(4)** personal preference. Most of the time none of these actually conflict — clarity and compliance point the same way.

## § 01 — Our five principles

Every rule in this guide traces back to one of these. When in doubt, return to them.

1. **Clear over clever.** If the reader cannot do the thing — understand a right, agree, opt out, cancel, or reach us — the document has failed, however elegant it reads. Comprehension is the job.
2. **Plain, not dumbed down.** We write at a 6th–8th grade reading level (5th grade for health and safety-critical content) without losing legal precision. Plain language and accuracy are partners, not rivals.
3. **Respectful and direct.** We say "you" to the reader and "we" for ourselves. We name who must do what, in the active voice. We don't lecture, threaten, or hide behind "information may be disclosed."
4. **Honest by design.** We surface what a reasonable person would want to know, before they'd want to know it. We never bury, manipulate, or use dark patterns. Leaving is as easy as joining.
5. **Accessible to everyone.** Our documents meet WCAG 2.1 Level AA, work with assistive technology, come in the languages we market in, and are available in another format on request.

## § 03 — Standards we follow

Our rules aren't preferences. They line up with recognized authority, which is what lets an attorney sign off with confidence and what we can point to if asked.

**Plain language**

- **ISO 24495-1:2023** — the international plain-language standard. Its test: readers can *find* what they need, *understand* what they find, and *use* it.
- **Federal Plain Language Guidelines** and the **Plain Writing Act of 2010** — the US public-sector benchmark.
- **Bryan Garner** (*Legal Writing in Plain English*; *The Redbook*, 5th ed.) and **Joseph Kimble** — the leading modern legal-writing authorities.

**Reading-level targets**

| Content | Target | Measure with |
|---|---|---|
| General consumer text (terms, policies, disclaimers) | 6th–8th grade | Flesch–Kincaid Grade Level |
| Health & safety-critical / consent content | 5th–6th grade | Flesch–Kincaid + CDC Clear Communication Index |
| Professional-only annexes (rare) | Plain, no fixed grade | Editorial review |

> **Read the score, don't worship it**
> Readability formulas penalize numbered and bulleted clauses even when those make a document *easier* to use. Treat the grade level as one signal among several — pair it with a human read and, for health content, a structured rubric like the CDC Index. A clear document that scores half a grade high beats a high-scoring one no one can follow.

## § 04 — Words & grammar

### Use the active voice and name the actor

Say who does what. The passive voice hides responsibility and is the native tongue of evasive legal writing.

> **✗ Avoid** — `Personal information may be shared with third parties.`
>
> **✓ Use** — `We share your information with the partners listed in § 4.`

### Write to the reader

Use **"you"** for the reader and **"we / us / our"** for our organization. It is clearer, shorter, and warmer than "the User" and "the Company."

### Choose the right obligation word

One of the oldest problems in legal drafting is that "shall" can mean must, will, may, or nothing at all. The federal court rules removed it; so do we. Pick the word that says exactly what you mean.

| Use | For | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **must** | a duty / requirement | You **must** be 18 to open an account. |
| **must not** | a prohibition | You **must not** resell the service. |
| **will** | our promise or a future fact | We **will** respond within 30 days. |
| **may** | permission or discretion | You **may** cancel at any time. |
| ~~shall~~ | — | Avoid. The only exception is verbatim statutory or mandated form text (log it — see § 17). |

### Keep sentences and paragraphs short

- Aim for an average sentence under **20 words**. One idea per sentence.
- Keep paragraphs to **five sentences or fewer**. Break a long obligation into a lead-in line plus a bulleted list.
- Prefer strong verbs over noun-strings: ~~make a determination~~ → **decide**.
- Avoid stacked negatives: ~~not uncommon~~ → **common**.

---

## § 05 — Banished legalese

These words add nothing but distance. Every modern legal-writing authority agrees: cut them. The list below is banned by default; keep one only under the precision exception (§ 17).

**Archaic pointers** — ~~herein, hereof, hereto, hereunder, hereinafter, thereof, thereto, whereby, wherein~~ — they're imprecise (which part do they point to?). Use **this Policy**, **this section**, **of it**, **under it**.

**Recital flourishes** — ~~WITNESSETH, WHEREAS, NOW THEREFORE, IN WITNESS WHEREOF~~ — delete them. Replace a wall of "whereas" clauses with a short plain "Background" sentence.

**Fake-precise pointers** — ~~said agreement, such party, the aforesaid, aforementioned~~ — use **the**, **this**, **that**.

**Coupled synonyms** — ~~null and void, indemnify and hold harmless, terms and conditions, any and all, each and every, force and effect~~ — pick the one word that carries the meaning.

> **⚠ Two careful exceptions**
>
> **Terms of art.** A few phrases (for example *indemnify and hold harmless*, *best efforts*, *consequential damages*) have settled meaning from prior court decisions. Don't "plain-language" these away on your own — flag them for the attorney, who may keep them under the precision exception.
>
> **Required all-caps.** A small set of disclosures (such as certain warranty disclaimers) must by law be conspicuous, sometimes in capitals. Keep caps only where the law requires; otherwise use **bold** or a callout box, which is more readable and still "conspicuous."

## § 07 — Defined terms

- **Define only when it earns its place.** A defined term should save real repetition or remove real ambiguity. Don't define a word you use twice.
- **Define in plain words, once,** on first use — either inline (capitalized, in parentheses) or in a short glossary near the top for longer documents.
- **Then use the term consistently.** Never switch between "Services," "Platform," and "site" for the same thing.
- **Capitalize defined terms** so the reader can see they carry a specific meaning (the *Services*, your *Account*).
- **Don't smuggle obligations into a definition.** Definitions say what a word means; they don't make rules.

---

## § 08 — Numbers, dates, money

- **Numerals for anything operative** — amounts, days, ages, percentages: "within 30 days," "you must be 18." They're easier to scan and harder to misread.
- **Drop the "words (numerals)" doubling.** Write **30 days**, not ~~thirty (30) days~~, unless a mandated form requires both.
- **Write dates unambiguously:** **June 7, 2026** (or ISO **2026-06-07**), never ~~6/7/26~~, which means different things in different countries.
- **Currency with the code where audiences are mixed:** "USD 49" or "$49 (USD)."
- **Express risk and frequency in plain terms** the reader can picture; avoid bare statistics in health content without a concrete frame.

---

## § 09 — Inclusive language

We frame this as a matter of **clarity and respect**, which is universally defensible: precise, person-respecting language is simply better legal writing.

- Use singular **"they"** for an unknown or unspecified person. Avoid "he/she" and "s/he."
- Prefer the plural to sidestep the issue entirely: "**users manage their accounts**" beats "~~the user manages his account~~."
- Drop gendered defaults: ~~chairman, manpower~~ → **chair, staff / workforce**.
- Don't assume race, ability, family structure, or marital status where it isn't relevant.
- For groups, use the term the community uses; follow current usage on capitalizing identity terms.

---

## § 10 — Layered structure

People skim. Give them a way in. Every footer document uses a **layered** shape: a short plain-language summary up top, then the full, legally complete text below. The summary informs; the full text governs — and we say so.

**The document skeleton**

1. **Title** — what it is, in the words people use ("Privacy Policy," not "Data Processing Instrument").
2. **Effective date** and **Last updated** date, shown at the top.
3. **Plain-language summary** — 3–5 sentences or a short bullet list (the "layer one" notice).
4. **Table of contents** with in-page anchor links.
5. **Numbered sections** with descriptive headings ("3. What we collect," not "3. Information").
6. **Defined terms** — inline on first use, or a short glossary for longer documents.
7. **How to contact us / exercise rights** — with every channel the law requires.
8. **Change log** — date plus a one-line description of each material change.
9. **Print / download** option and a language switcher where relevant.

> **Summaries help, but the full text controls**
> A summary is a courtesy, not a loophole. Make sure it's accurate and add a short line such as: "This summary is here to help. The full terms below are what apply." Never let the summary contradict or soften what the full text actually does.

---

## § 11 — Formatting

- **Headings:** sentence case, descriptive, nested no deeper than three levels in body content.
- **Numbering:** tiered decimal (1, 1.1, 1.1.1). Avoid the Roman→letter→numeral cascade that no one can cite.
- **Lists:** use them for anything with three or more parallel items. Bullets for sets, numbers for sequence or things you'll cross-reference.
- **Cross-references** by section number ("see § 4"), never "as set forth herein."
- **Body text:** at least 16px on the web; 12-point minimum in print/PDF.
- **Links:** describe the destination ("read our Cookie Policy"), never "click here."
- **Tables** get a caption and real column headers so screen readers can navigate them.
- **All-caps blocks** only where a law requires them; otherwise bold or a callout box.

---

## § 12 — Accessibility

A legal notice no one can perceive is no notice at all. Our floor is **WCAG 2.1 Level AA**; we treat WCAG 2.2 AA as a stretch goal. This is the standard courts and regulators apply in practice, and federally funded health programs are held to it directly.

**The essentials for legal text**

- Real, semantic HTML headings in order — not bold text pretending to be a heading.
- Text contrast of at least 4.5:1 against its background.
- Meaning never carried by color alone (a red word also gets a label or icon).
- Resizable text and a reading order that survives zoom and reflow.
- Keyboard-reachable links and controls, with a visible focus state.
- A genuine plain-text or HTML version — not only a scanned-image PDF.

**The Accessibility Statement itself**

When we publish one, it states our conformance target (WCAG 2.1 AA), lists any known gaps honestly, gives a contact for accommodation requests, names the date last reviewed, and offers an alternative format on request.

## § 13 — Honesty & non-deception

Tone is not just wording; it's whether the design is fair. Regulators judge a disclosure by whether an ordinary person actually notices and understands it — the "clear and conspicuous" test — and they treat manipulative design ("dark patterns") as deceptive on its own.

**What this means for us**

- **Conspicuous, not buried.** Material terms sit where the reader will see them, near the choice they affect — not hidden in a footnote or behind a scroll.
- **Real consent.** No pre-checked boxes, no consent bundled with unrelated terms, no "I agree" that quietly covers more than it says.
- **Symmetry.** Cancelling, unsubscribing, or opting out is at least as easy as signing up was. Same number of steps, same prominence.
- **No false urgency or shame.** We don't use countdowns, guilt-worded decline buttons, or visual tricks that steer the choice.
- **Plain choices.** Options are labeled in plain words so the reader knows what each one does.

> **Why this lives in a tone guide**
> Because the same instinct that produces honest wording produces honest design. If a sentence only works when the reader misses it, rewrite the sentence — don't hide it.

## § 14 — Modular layers

The guide is built in layers so it stays reusable. Start with the universal layer, then switch on whichever others apply. At the top of each document, note which layers are in force.

**Universal — US baseline** · *Always on*
Everything in this guide: plain language, the five principles, banished legalese, layered structure, accessibility, and honest design. This is the floor for every document, every industry.

**California add-on** · *Default on*
California privacy law requires "plain, straightforward language" and points to the federal plain-language guidelines — so our defaults already align. Layer on its specific notices (notice at collection, the "Do Not Sell or Share" choice, the annual update with a 12-month look-back) and watch new obligations such as plain-language pre-use notices for automated decision-making.

**Healthcare add-on** · *First use*
HIPAA requires a Notice of Privacy Practices in plain language — historically the most-failed requirement, so it's exactly where this guide helps. Tighten the reading level to 5th–6th grade, check health content against the CDC Clear Communication Index, and track the current HHS model-notice content and deadlines.

**EU add-on** · *Future*
Not a default. Our plain-language and layered-notice habits already travel well to EU transparency expectations. When we expand, switch this on to address the additional region-specific disclosures rather than reworking the base voice.

> **A note on currency**
> Specific rules, thresholds, and deadlines change. This guide names the standards and the shape of the obligations; it is not the live text of any statute. Before publishing, confirm the current requirement with the supervising attorney. See the watch-list in § 17.

## § 15 — Worked example

The same clause, before and after. Nothing legally meaningful is lost — only the fog.

> **Before** · 58 words, ~grade 17
> *"In the event that the User shall wish to terminate the Account, the User must provide written notice to the Company prior to the expiration of the then-current billing period, whereupon the Company shall, notwithstanding the foregoing, retain such data as may be required pursuant to applicable law."*

> **After** · 36 words, ~grade 8
> *"You can close your account at any time. To avoid being charged for the next period, tell us before the current one ends. After you close it, we keep only the data the law requires us to keep."*

What changed: active voice, "you" and "we," "shall" gone, "notwithstanding the foregoing" and "pursuant to" gone, one idea per sentence. What stayed: the timing rule, the notice requirement, and the legal-retention carve-out.

## § 16 — Pre-publish checklist

Run this before any legal document goes live.

- [ ] **Reading level on target.** Flesch–Kincaid within range for the content type; health content also checked against a structured rubric.
- [ ] **"Shall" eliminated;** obligations use must / must not / will / may. Any surviving "shall" is mandated text and is logged.
- [ ] **Banished legalese removed** (herein, whereas, pursuant to, doublets). Surviving terms of art are flagged and approved.
- [ ] **Active voice; reader addressed as "you."**
- [ ] **Layered structure present:** summary, dates, TOC, descriptive headings, change log.
- [ ] **Accessibility verified:** semantic headings, 4.5:1 contrast, no color-only meaning, keyboard reachable, real text version.
- [ ] **Honest design:** material terms conspicuous, real consent, leaving as easy as joining, no dark patterns.
- [ ] **Applicable layers applied** (California by default; industry and region as needed) and current requirements confirmed.
- [ ] **Mandated elements present** for the document type (e.g., required notices and contact channels).
- [ ] **Supervising attorney has reviewed and signed off;** sign-off recorded in the change log.

# Research Foundations for a General-Purpose "Legal Tone of Voice" Style Guide

## TL;DR
- **Build the guide around four pillars** that are now backed by enforceable law, an international standard, and modern legal-writing authority: (1) **plain language** (Plain Writing Act of 2010, Federal Plain Language Guidelines, ISO 24495-1:2023, Bryan Garner, Joseph Kimble); (2) **transparency and non-deception** (FTC "clear and conspicuous" doctrine, CCPA "plain, straightforward language" rule at 11 CCR § 7003, dark-patterns regulations); (3) **accessibility** (WCAG 2.1 Level AA as the de facto US legal floor for both public and private sites in 2026); and (4) **modular jurisdictional layers** with US/federal defaults, a California baseline (CCPA as amended, 2026 regulations), an industry layer (HIPAA NPP plain-language mandate, CDC/AHRQ health-literacy guidance), and headroom for future EU/GDPR use.
- **The strongest, most defensible drafting rules** for the guide: write consumer text at a 6th-to-8th-grade reading level (5th grade for safety-critical and health-consent material per AHRQ and The Joint Commission); replace "shall" with "must," "will," or "may" (the federal courts have purged "shall" from the Federal Rules of Civil Procedure, Evidence, Appellate, and Criminal Rules); banish archaic legalese (hereinafter, witnesseth, whereas, aforesaid, said, such, pursuant to); use active voice, short sentences, second person ("you"), defined terms only when truly necessary; use a layered notice structure (short summary + full legal text) with an "Effective date," a "Last updated" date, and a changelog; ensure WCAG 2.1 AA conformance plus an Accessibility Statement.
- **Treat the guide as a living document** with three modular sections — Universal (US federal/common-law baseline), California Add-On (CCPA, CPRA, CPPA 2026 rules, ADMT pre-use notices), and Healthcare Add-On (HIPAA NPP, CDC Clear Communication Index, AHRQ Universal Precautions Toolkit). Add an optional EU module later. Anchor every rule to an authoritative citation so the supervising attorneys signing off feel covered, and supply do/don't examples so non-lawyer writers can apply rules without judgment calls.

## Key Findings

### 1. Plain language is now a written standard, not a preference
- The **Plain Writing Act of 2010** (Pub. L. 111-274) requires US federal agencies to use "clear Government communication that the public can understand and use," and the **Federal Plain Language Guidelines** at plainlanguage.gov are the operative implementation document. Agencies continue to file Plain Writing Act compliance reports annually (PCLOB filed its 2026 report on April 16, 2026; FEC on April 13, 2026; OSHRC on April 11, 2025). Multiple federal agencies (Treasury, DOJ, Department of Education, FCC, GSA) have institutionalized these guidelines, which lets a Legal Tone of Voice guide cite them as a public-sector gold standard.
- **ISO 24495-1:2023 ("Plain language — Part 1: Governing principles and guidelines"),** published June 27, 2023 and developed by ISO/TC 37 with experts from 25 countries representing 19 languages, is the first international plain-language standard. It defines plain language as communication in which "wording, structure and design are so clear that intended readers can easily — find what they need, understand what they find, and use that information." The four governing principles are **relevance, findability, understanding, and usability.** Adopting ISO 24495-1 by reference gives a private-sector style guide an external, neutral authority.
- The **California Consumer Privacy Act regulations** explicitly require "plain, straightforward language" in privacy notices and notices at collection (11 CCR § 7003), and point businesses to the Federal Plain Language Guidelines as a reference standard. California has effectively imported federal plain-language norms into private-sector consumer disclosures.
- **HIPAA's Notice of Privacy Practices rule** (45 CFR § 164.520(b)) requires NPPs to be "written in plain language." OCR has interpreted this to mean: organize material to serve the reader; write short sentences in the active voice using "you" and other pronouns; use common, everyday words; and divide material into short sections. OCR explicitly permits "layered notices" — a short summary plus the full required content.
- **State insurance readability statutes** make plain language legally mandatory in another context: Florida (Fla. Stat. § 627.4145) requires a Flesch Reading Ease score ≥ 45; Texas, Arkansas, Hawaii, North Dakota, Ohio, Oklahoma, South Dakota, and Tennessee require ≥ 40; Maine, Massachusetts, Minnesota, Montana, Nebraska, Nevada, New Jersey, New Mexico, and North Carolina require ≥ 50; Rhode Island requires an 8th-grade Flesch-Kincaid grade level or below and 12-point type for health insurance policies; South Carolina requires life insurance disclosures at a Flesch-Kincaid grade level of 7th grade or lower.

### 2. Reading-level targets: what to write into the guide
- **General consumer content: 6th to 8th-grade reading level.** This is consistent with the state insurance-readability statutes (most converge near an 8th–9th grade level) and the broader Federal Plain Language Guidelines.
- **Health-related/patient-facing content: target 6th grade, with a stretch goal of 5th grade.** AHRQ's *Health Literacy Universal Precautions Toolkit* (2nd ed., Tool 11) states: "As one-fifth of adults read below the 5th grade level… to ensure wide understanding, it is best for materials to be [written at the 5th grade level or below]." AHRQ's PSNet primer recommends "4th to 6th grade level." A widely cited NIH/AMA joint recommendation (Stossel et al., *J Med Libr Assoc*, 2012; PMC5764592) is that patient-education materials "be written at or below a sixth-grade reading level." The Joint Commission also recommends 5th grade or lower for safety-critical patient materials.
- **Tools to measure:** Flesch Reading Ease and Flesch-Kincaid Grade Level (built into Microsoft Word); the CDC Clear Communication Index (research-based scoring rubric); and the AHRQ Patient Education Materials Assessment Tool (PEMAT). Note that contract-drafting expert Ken Adams cautions that highly structured legal documents with enumerated clauses tend to score poorly on Flesch tests even when very readable, so readability scores should be one signal, not a single pass/fail.

### 3. Legal-writing authority converges on the same do/don't list
The two most-cited modern authorities on US legal writing are **Bryan A. Garner** (editor-in-chief of *Black's Law Dictionary*; author of *Legal Writing in Plain English* (3rd ed., 2023), *The Redbook: A Manual on Legal Style* (5th ed., 2023), and *Garner's Modern English Usage*) and **Joseph Kimble** (Distinguished Professor Emeritus, Cooley Law School; author of *Lifting the Fog of Legalese* (2006) and *Seeing Through Legalese* (2017); drafting consultant to the U.S. Judicial Conference). Their core prescriptions, which a Tone of Voice guide should adopt verbatim:

- **Use plain English, not legalese.** Avoid pretension, officialese, and stiff formulas. (Garner §§ 12–13)
- **Use strong, precise verbs; minimize "is/are/was/were" and passive voice.** (Garner § 9)
- **Avoid multiple negatives.** (Garner § 10)
- **Simplify wordy phrases (especially those with "of") and avoid "zombie nouns"** — -ion words you can turn into verbs. (Garner §§ 14–15)
- **Avoid doublets and triplets** ("null and void," "indemnify and hold harmless," "due and owing," "full force and effect," "free and clear"). The *Redbook* (5th ed., 2023) lists roughly 500 such legalisms with plain-English equivalents.
- **Refer to people and companies by name. Never use -or/-ee terms ("Licensor/Licensee," "Indemnitor/Indemnitee"). Shun unfamiliar acronyms.** (Garner § 17)
- **Be a "companionable voice of reason." Make everything you write speakable.** (Garner § 20)

### 4. The "shall" question — settled in favor of "must" (mostly)
The Federal Rules of Civil Procedure, Evidence, Appellate, and Criminal Procedure have been restyled to eliminate "shall." The Advisory Committee Note explains: "the word 'shall' can mean 'must,' 'may,' or something else, depending on context… The restyled rules replace 'shall' with 'must,' 'may,' or 'should.'" The **Federal Plain Language Guidelines describe "shall" as "officious" and "obsolete"** and direct drafters to use "must" for obligations. Bryan Garner ("Shall We Abandon Shall?" *ABA Journal*, August 2012) advocates the "ABC rule" (Australian/British/Canadian) — delete every "shall."

- **Tone of Voice rule:** use **must** for obligations, **will** for future events or company promises, **may** for permission/discretion, and **must not** for prohibitions. Garner himself notes that in **consumer contracts specifically**, the consumer's obligations should be expressed as "must" (not "will" or "shall"), because consumer contracts are take-it-or-leave-it.
- **Treat "shall" as banned by default**, with a narrow exception when the term is mandated verbatim by a statute or required regulatory form.

### 5. Banished words and phrases (every legal-writing authority agrees)
- Archaic adverbials: **hereinafter, hereto, herein, hereof, hereunder, heretofore, hereafter, thereof, thereto, therefrom, thereunder, whereby, whereof, wherein.** Garner: these words "defy precision because it is never clear whether they are referencing a particular paragraph, section, or the entire agreement."
- Recital archaisms: **WHEREAS, NOW THEREFORE, WITNESSETH, IN WITNESS WHEREOF.** Garner: witnesseth "is a traditional but worthless flourish… best deleted in modern contracts."
- Pointing words misused: **said, such, aforementioned, aforesaid** — replace with *the, this, that, those.*
- Inflated phrases: **pursuant to** (→ under, according to); **prior to** (→ before); **subsequent to** (→ after); **in the event that** (→ if); **with respect to** (→ about); **notwithstanding the foregoing** (→ even so / despite this); **provided, however, that** (→ but).
- Coupled synonyms (doublets/triplets): **null and void, indemnify and hold harmless, terms and conditions, force and effect, free and clear, any and all, each and every** — pick one word.
- Latin/Law French (avoid unless the term is genuinely irreplaceable): inter alia, ipso facto, mutatis mutandis, ab initio, sui generis, simpliciter.
- Distancing constructions: "the party of the first part," "comes now," "to the Honorable Court," "(hereinafter 'Acme')."
- All-caps blocks (except for the small set of disclosures the law genuinely requires in all caps, like certain UCC § 2-316 warranty disclaimers). All-caps reduces readability; bold or a callout box achieves "conspicuous" status more effectively.

### 6. FTC "clear and conspicuous" doctrine — the federal anti-deception baseline
Section 5 of the FTC Act prohibits "unfair or deceptive acts or practices." Two FTC instruments specifically govern how legal disclosures must look and read on consumer websites:

- **FTC Endorsement Guides (revised June 2023)** define "clear and conspicuous" as a disclosure that is "difficult to miss (i.e., easily noticeable) and easily understandable by ordinary consumers." Disclosures should be unavoidable, near the claim, and not buried in legalese.
- **FTC Negative Option Rule / "Click-to-Cancel" Rule** (16 CFR Part 425, finalized October 16, 2024). The Eighth Circuit vacated this rule on procedural grounds in *Custom Communications, Inc. v. Federal Trade Commission*, No. 24-3137, 2025 WL 1873489 (8th Cir. July 8, 2025); a unanimous three-judge panel held the FTC's "rulemaking process was procedurally insufficient" for failing to issue a required preliminary regulatory analysis under 15 U.S.C. § 57b-3. The FTC has confirmed it will continue enforcement under existing Section 5 and ROSCA authority. The substantive principles remain enforceable: clear-and-conspicuous disclosure of material terms; express informed consent (no pre-checked boxes); a cancellation mechanism at least as easy as enrollment ("symmetry in choice"). On **March 11, 2026, the FTC published an Advance Notice of Proposed Rulemaking** titled "Rule Concerning the Use of Prenotification Negative Option Plans (Negative Option Rule); Advance Notice of Proposed Rulemaking; Request for Public Comment," 91 Fed. Reg. (Mar. 13, 2026), signaling a replacement rule with a public comment deadline of April 13, 2026.
- **Dark patterns:** the FTC's 2022 staff report classifies "design elements that hide or delay disclosure of material information" as deceptive. The CCPA Regulations independently define and ban dark patterns at 11 CCR § 7004; consent obtained through a dark pattern is "void." The **Amazon Prime $2.5 billion settlement** is the marquee enforcement action — the settlement order was entered September 25, 2025, by Judge John H. Chun (U.S. District Court, W.D. Wash.) and comprises $1 billion in civil penalties (the largest ever in an FTC rule-violation case) and $1.5 billion in consumer redress to approximately 35 million affected consumers.

### 7. California — the operative US baseline (CCPA/CPRA 2026)
- The **2025 CCPA/CPRA regulations** were unanimously adopted by the CPPA Board on July 24, 2025, approved by the Office of Administrative Law on September 22, 2025, and take effect **January 1, 2026.** They introduce: cybersecurity audit requirements; risk assessments for high-risk processing; rules on Automated Decisionmaking Technology (ADMT) — including a **Pre-Use Notice in plain language** describing the specific purpose and opt-out choices when ADMT is used for "significant decisions" affecting consumers/employees (effective Jan. 1, 2027); clarifications for insurance companies; and revisions to opt-out preference signal handling.
- **2025 monetary thresholds:** the law applies if annual gross revenue ≥ $26,625,000 (CPI-adjusted from $25M); the business processes 100,000+ California residents/households; or it derives ≥ 50% of revenue from selling/sharing personal information. Statutory damages adjusted to $107–$799 per consumer per incident effective Jan. 1, 2025.
- **Notice content requirements** (CCPA Regulations § 7011 et seq.): the privacy policy must "use plain, straightforward language and avoid technical or legal jargon"; be available in the languages the business uses for other consumer-facing content; be reasonably accessible to consumers with disabilities (following WCAG); be printable; be updated at least every 12 months with the prior 12-month look-back; and accessible via a conspicuous link from the homepage and any app settings menu.
- **CPPA enforcement is active.** Notable 2025 actions: the **Tractor Supply Company $1.35 million settlement, announced September 30, 2025, was "the largest in the agency's brief history"** per the CPPA's own press release (cppa.ca.gov/announcements/2025/20250930.html), and was the CPPA's first enforcement action specifically addressing job-applicant privacy rights, citing failure to update the posted privacy notice annually. Multiple data-broker settlements under the Delete Act. The CPPA also issued Enforcement Advisory No. 2025-01 on data brokers and dark patterns. The CPPA opened a Data Broker Enforcement Strike Force in November 2025.

### 8. Healthcare layer — HIPAA NPP, OCR, and health literacy
- **HIPAA Notice of Privacy Practices** (45 CFR § 164.520) must be written in plain language and contain specific elements (uses/disclosures; individual rights; entity duties; complaint contacts; effective date). OCR's most recent model NPPs were updated in **February 2026** to incorporate revised content on **Substance Use Disorder (Part 2) records** as required by the 42 CFR Part 2 final rule (89 Fed. Reg. 33064, April 26, 2024). Compliance deadline: **February 16, 2026.** OCR also launched a civil enforcement program for confidentiality of SUD records on that date.
- A key compliance gap: **OCR's 2016–2017 HIPAA Audits Industry Report (Phase II audits of 166 covered entities and 41 business associates) found that "Of the covered entities audited, only 2% fully met the content requirements of a valid NPP. Most covered entities failed to provide required content related to individual rights or, in some cases, failed to provide an NPP written in plain language."** The plain-language mandate is the single most-failed element. This is a market signal that a credible plain-language Tone of Voice product has demand.
- **Health literacy benchmarks:** AHRQ Universal Precautions Toolkit (Tool 11) — aim for **5th grade level or below.** AMA/NIH consensus — **6th grade or below.** The Joint Commission — **5th grade or below** for safety-critical content. The CDC Clear Communication Index supplies a 4-part, evidence-based scoring rubric (main message and call to action; language; information design; state of the science; behavioral recommendations; numbers; risk).

### 9. Accessibility — WCAG 2.1 Level AA is the operative US legal floor
- **DOJ ADA Title II final rule** (89 Fed. Reg. 31320, April 24, 2024) adopts **WCAG 2.1 Level AA** as the technical standard for state and local government web content and mobile apps. Compliance dates were extended by the April 20, 2026 Interim Final Rule: April 26, 2027 (population ≥ 50,000) and April 26, 2028 (smaller entities and special districts).
- **ADA Title III** (private businesses, including private hospitals, clinics, and medical offices as "places of public accommodation" per ada.gov) — no DOJ web rule exists yet. The 2017 Title III rulemaking remains paused, and DOJ announced in October 2025 it would "re-examine" ADA regulations under Executive Order 14192 (Unleashing Prosperity Through Deregulation, Jan. 31, 2025). However, **courts and DOJ enforcement settlements consistently apply WCAG 2.1 Level AA as the de facto Title III standard.** Private healthcare entities should commit to WCAG 2.1 AA in their Accessibility Statement (with WCAG 2.2 AA as a stretch goal — it is backward-compatible).
- **HHS Section 504 final rule** (89 Fed. Reg. 40066, May 9, 2024) imposes WCAG 2.1 AA on federally funded health programs and activities; the compliance deadline was extended in 2026 to **May 11, 2027.** Any healthcare provider that takes Medicare or Medicaid is directly within this rule.
- **Accessibility Statement content** (industry consensus): conformance target (WCAG 2.1 AA); known limitations; feedback contact / accommodation request mechanism; date last reviewed; alternative-format availability.

### 10. Layered notices, structure, and version control
Empirical research from the Nielsen Norman Group (NN/g) and IAPP, plus published Article 29 Working Party guidance, supports the **layered notice** pattern: a short, plain-language summary at the top of the document, followed by the full, legally complete text below, with an in-page table of contents. NN/g's research finds users expect: a high-level overview; the date last updated; a summary of recent changes (not just "we updated this"); and consistent definitions used the same way throughout.

- **Structural conventions for online legal documents:** Title at top; "Effective date" + "Last updated" date prominently displayed; in-page table of contents with anchor links; numbered sections with descriptive (not generic) headings; defined-terms section near the top OR inline italicized definitions on first use; bulleted lists for parallel items; short paragraphs (≤ 5 sentences); cross-references by section number not by "herein"; printable view available; archived prior versions linked.

### 11. Inclusive and bias-conscious language
The 5th edition of Garner's *The Redbook* (West Academic, 2023) explicitly adds material on **pronoun use, particularly singular "they," and questions of unconscious bias.** Modern legal-style consensus:

- Use **singular "they"** for unknown or unspecified persons. Avoid "he/she," "(s)he," "s/he."
- Prefer plural nouns and pronouns: "users have their accounts" rather than "the user has his account."
- Avoid gendered defaults ("chairman," "manpower" → "chair," "workforce/staff"). Avoid generic "he."
- Avoid identity defaults that assume race, ability, family structure, or marital status.
- Capitalize "Black" when referring to people; follow current AP/Chicago/Redbook guidance on "White."
- Use person-first or identity-first language as the affected community prefers (e.g., "person with a disability" vs. "disabled person"; "people with diabetes" not "diabetics").
- The Linguistic Society of America's Guidelines for Inclusive Language is a reasonable external authority to cite.

## Details — Concrete Building Blocks for the Style Guide

### A. Voice and tone principles (5 numbered principles)
Recommended formulation, drawn from ISO 24495-1, the Federal Plain Language Guidelines, and Mailchimp/Shopify-style brand-voice patterns adapted to legal text:

1. **Clear over clever.** If a reader cannot do what they need to do — understand a right, exercise an option, agree, opt out, or contact us — the document has failed, no matter how legally elegant.
2. **Plain, not dumbed down.** We write at a 6th–8th grade reading level (5th grade for safety-critical and health-consent material) without sacrificing legal precision. When law requires a defined term, we define it once, in plain words, and use it consistently.
3. **Respectful and direct.** We use "you" to address the reader and "we" to refer to the company. We do not lecture, threaten, or hide behind passive voice ("information may be disclosed" → "we may share your information").
4. **Honest by design.** We disclose the things a reasonable reader would want to know before they would want to know them. We do not bury, manipulate, or dark-pattern. Cancellation, opt-out, and contact paths are at least as easy as the path that got the user in.
5. **Accessible to everyone.** Our documents conform to WCAG 2.1 Level AA, are available in the languages we use for marketing in the same market, and offer alternative formats on request.

A useful "this but never that" formulation:
- *Authoritative, but never aggressive.*
- *Precise, but never pedantic.*
- *Formal enough to be trusted, casual enough to be read.*
- *Comprehensive, but never padded.*
- *Cautious, but never evasive.*

### C. Structural template for a footer legal document
1. **Title** (Privacy Policy / Terms of Service / etc.)
2. **Effective date** and **Last updated** date
3. **Plain-language summary** (3–5 sentences or 5–8 bullets — the "layer 1" notice)
4. **Table of contents** with anchor links
5. **Numbered sections** with descriptive headings (e.g., "3. What we collect," not "3. Information")
6. **Defined terms** either in a short glossary or inline on first use (italic + capitalized)
7. **Contact section** with email, mailing address, and (for healthcare/CCPA) toll-free number and complaint mechanism
8. **Changelog** showing version history (date + one-sentence description of each material change)
9. **Print/PDF link** and language switcher (where applicable)

### D. Formatting rules
- Sentences: aim for ≤ 20 words on average. Paragraphs: ≤ 5 sentences.
- Body text: minimum 16px on the web; 12-point in PDF (Rhode Island's health insurance regulation uses 12-point as a minimum).
- Headings: sentence case; descriptive; nested no deeper than H3 in body content.
- Numbering: tiered decimal (1, 1.1, 1.1.1) — avoid the Roman→Letter→Arabic→roman-numeral cascade.
- Defined terms: capitalize and italicize on first use, then capitalize on subsequent use ("Services").
- Lists: prefer bullets to numbered lists unless order or cross-reference matters.
- Tables: always have a caption and column headers for screen-reader users.
- All-caps blocks: only where statutorily required (e.g., warranty disclaimers, jury-trial waivers in some states). Otherwise use bold + a callout box.
- Links: descriptive text (not "click here"); underlined; sufficient color contrast (WCAG 2.1 SC 1.4.3, ratio ≥ 4.5:1).

### E. Governance and maintenance
- Designate a single owner (e.g., General Counsel + Head of Content/Design as co-owners).
- Annual review cycle (CCPA requires the privacy policy to be updated at least every 12 months; the Tractor Supply enforcement action cited failure to do so).
- Plain-language quality gate: run Flesch-Kincaid before publication; require all consumer-facing legal documents to score within target.
- CDC Clear Communication Index check for health content.
- Accessibility audit (manual + automated) before publication and at each material revision.
- Subject-matter attorney sign-off recorded in the changelog.

- If readability scores creep above the target by more than half a grade level on a rolling sample, run a remediation sprint.

## Caveats

- **Plain language and legal enforceability can conflict at the margins.** When a court has already interpreted a term of art ("indemnify and hold harmless," "best efforts," "consequential damages") in a known way, replacing it with a plain-English equivalent may invite litigation risk. The guide should include an explicit "legal-precision exception" that lets the reviewing attorney retain a defined term of art *if and only if* it is documented in the changelog with a reason. Bryan Garner's own position is that this exception should be narrow.
- **Readability scores are crude.** Flesch-Kincaid penalizes long, enumerated clauses even when those clauses are actually easier to read because they are bulleted. Use the scores as a signal, not a verdict. Pair them with at least one structured rubric (CDC Clear Communication Index for health; AHRQ PEMAT for patient materials).
- **The FTC Click-to-Cancel rule was vacated** by the Eighth Circuit in *Custom Communications, Inc. v. FTC*, No. 24-3137, 2025 WL 1873489 (8th Cir. July 8, 2025) on procedural grounds. The substantive principles remain enforceable under Section 5 and ROSCA, and the FTC has signaled a replacement rule (ANPRM, 91 Fed. Reg. (Mar. 13, 2026)). Build the guide to the substantive principles, not the vacated rule's specific text.
- **DOJ has paused both ADA Title II and Title III rulemaking.** In October 2025 DOJ announced it would "re-examine" ADA regulations under Executive Order 14192. The Title II compliance dates have been pushed to April 26, 2027 / 2028. WCAG 2.1 AA remains the operative standard, but expect movement. The guide should commit to WCAG 2.1 AA as the floor while monitoring for change.
- **HIPAA was partially reshaped by litigation.** *Purl v. HHS* (2025) vacated most of the 2024 HIPAA reproductive-health-care privacy rule. The Part 2-related NPP revisions survived and were required by Feb. 16, 2026. The guide's healthcare module should track HIPAA litigation closely.
- **California regulations have multiple effective dates rolling through 2027–2030.** The base 2025 regulations are effective January 1, 2026; ADMT pre-use notice obligations effective January 1, 2027; cybersecurity audit certifications due to the CPPA starting April 1, 2028. Keep a compliance-date table in the guide.
- **Inclusive language is contested terrain.** Some jurisdictions and audiences (notably certain federal agencies under current administration policy) are pulling back from DEI-coded language. The guide's bias-conscious-language section should be framed in clarity-and-respect terms (which are universally defensible) rather than ideology terms.
- **Garner and other style authorities sometimes disagree** with practicing contract drafters (notably Ken Adams of *A Manual of Style for Contract Drafting*) on edge cases — most famously whether to fully abolish "shall." Where authorities disagree, pick a single rule for the guide and cite both sides, so attorneys reviewing know the trade-off was deliberate.

**Purpose:** This style guide establishes the definitive framework for all user-facing legal documents, terms of service, privacy policies, and disclaimers. Our legal tone is defined as **"professional but approachable"** and **"authoritative and neutral"**. We do not want consumers to feel alienated or intimidated when reading our terms.

* **Be Human:** Use contractions (e.g., "we're," "it's," "don't") to make the writing sound more natural and to flow with the rest of our brand voice. Using contractions does not affect the legal validity of our agreements.


* **Use Friendly Pronouns:** Define the parties upfront using standard pronouns (e.g., *"The Company ('we,' 'us,' or 'our') and you, the user ('you' or 'your')"*) and stick to them. Avoid referring to users as "The User" or "The Customer" throughout the text.


* **Avoid Buzzwords & Idioms:** Do not use business jargon like "synergy" or "bandwidth," and avoid regional colloquialisms that could confuse non-native speakers or international users.



## 2. Formatting and Structural Engineering

Before writing, plan the document so that the most important details are immediately visible to the reader.

* **Informative Headings:** Use clear, descriptive markers for sections and subsections (e.g., "How to Terminate Your Account" instead of "General Provisions").


* **Deep Issue Framing:** Spill the crucial details immediately on the first page; do not bury the core premise under pages of preamble.


* **Signposting and White Space:** Break complex concepts into vertical lists or bullet points to make them easy to scan.



## 3. The "Conspicuous" Rule (No ALL-CAPS)

Historically, lawyers used all-caps to make liability and warranty clauses "conspicuous." We **do not** use all-caps.

* **The Habituation Trap:** Empirical research shows that all-caps does not improve consumer consent and actively diminishes reading comprehension, especially for users over age 55. It acts as "textual yelling".


* **Alternative Highlighting:** To meet the FTC's standard for clear and conspicuous disclosures—which is a performance standard measured by whether consumers actually notice and understand it—use standard sentence case. Highlight critical terms by using **bold text** sparingly or by placing the clause inside a distinct visual box.



## 4. Sentence Mechanics and Syntactic Economy

* **The 20-Word Rule:** Keep your average sentence length to about 20 words or less. This helps ensure the document meets the Grade 8 reading level standard mandated by California equity standards for public-facing documents.


* **Active Voice:** Always prefer the active voice (Subject + Verb + Object). Say, "You must pay the fee," rather than, "The fee must be paid," to clearly establish who bears the obligation.


* **Subject-Verb Proximity:** Keep the subject, verb, and object close together at the beginning of the sentence to minimize cognitive load.


* **Positive Phrasing:** Avoid multiple negatives. Instead of "Notice will not be effective unless," use "Notice is effective only if".



## 5. Vocabulary and "Legalese" Banishment

"Legaldegook" creates artificial distance and must be excised. Good legal writing is simply ordinary, well-written English.

* **Banned Words:** Completely avoid archaic terms like *heretofore, pursuant to, aforementioned, witnesseth, hereby, herein,* and *to wit*.


* **Replace "Such" and "Said":** Do not use "such" or "said" as adjectives (e.g., "said property"). Use *the, this, that, these,* or *those*.


* **Eradicate "Shall":** The word "shall" is notoriously ambiguous. Delete every instance of it. Use "must" for obligations, "will" for future events, and "may" for permission.


* **Fix Nominalizations:** Turn nouns masquerading as verbs back into action verbs (e.g., change "make an agreement" to "agree," and "remit payment" to "pay").



## 6. Definitions and Context

* **Inline Explanations:** If a legal term of art is absolutely unavoidable (e.g., "represent and warrant"), provide a quick, real-world example inline rather than relying on a separate "plain language summary" column.


* **Minimize Definitions:** If you have just a few definitions, explain them in plain language at the start of the document. If the document requires many definitions, move them to a schedule at the end so they don't block the user from reading the actual agreement.

# **Legal Tone of Voice and Document Drafting Standards: A Comprehensive Framework for Clarity, Compliance, and User Trust**

## **The Paradigm Shift in Legal Document Drafting**

The landscape of legal document drafting has undergone a fundamental paradigm shift. For centuries, legal writing was characterized by archaic terminology, convoluted sentence structures, and an intentional density designed to insulate documents from varying interpretations. However, the movement toward a "plain English" or "plain language" standard—which began in earnest in the 1970s—has redefined the benchmark for enforceable, professional legal communication.1  
The premise of modern legal drafting is remarkably straightforward: good legal writing should not differ, without compelling reason, from ordinary, well-written English.1 Legal minds often become deadened by the turgidity of traditional language, allowing unintended slips and errors in judgment to go undetected.2 Plain language does not inherently guarantee a sound underlying legal solution, but it makes errors more visible and complicated ideas significantly clearer.2 Furthermore, a difficult subject does not need to be compounded by a difficult style; good legal writing is merely literary English applied to the law.1  
In highly regulated digital environments—such as healthcare platforms, consumer financial services, and corporate websites—intentionally obfuscated legalese is now viewed as an active liability rather than a protective shield.1 Businesses have found that clearly written consumer contracts reduce costly defaults, decrease customer support burdens, and project a brand value rooted in transparency and trust.1 Consequently, federal regulators, state legislatures, and the judiciary increasingly demand that consumer-facing legal documents, disclosures, and privacy policies be drafted in a manner that the average reader can readily comprehend.3  
This comprehensive analysis establishes a modernized Legal Tone of Voice and drafting framework. It is strategically calibrated for digital environments, specifically for legal documents displayed as clickable links in website footers within highly regulated industries such as healthcare. The framework establishes a default jurisdictional baseline of California and the broader United States, while deliberately maintaining a high-level architectural structure that remains strictly compatible with forthcoming European digital accessibility and transparency standards.

## **Defining the Audience and Jurisdictional Scope**

The primary reader for general-purpose website legal documents consists of a multifaceted audience: everyday users interacting with the platform, alongside legal professionals reviewing the terms for compliance or litigation purposes. This dichotomy requires a drafting approach that abandons the myth of writing for a hypothetical judge who might someday review the document, and instead focuses on the practical comprehension of the end-user.5  
Drafting in a manner that intentionally obfuscates terms through dense legalese wastes the time of all parties involved and sends a detrimental signal regarding corporate values. A modern corporate voice must bridge the gap between strict legal compliance and the company's overarching brand identity. Consumers should not feel as though they have crossed a hostile, bureaucratic border when they navigate from a company's marketing pages to its terms of service.6  
The jurisdictional strategy for this framework is anchored in California law by default, representing the strictest domestic consumer protection and privacy standards in the United States. Adhering to California's requirements inherently establishes a baseline of compliance that satisfies the vast majority of other state and federal requirements. Simultaneously, the framework integrates the structural tenets of the European Union’s General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR) and the European Accessibility Act (EAA) as a future-proofing measure. While EU compliance is not the immediate default, drafting documents that are fundamentally inaccessible or structurally incompatible with EU transparency mandates would require costly, ground-up revisions during future market expansions. Therefore, the tone of voice must be universally intelligible.

## **Foundational Principles of Plain English Legal Drafting**

The foundation of a modern legal tone of voice relies on structural engineering, syntactic economy, and lexical precision. The standard for producing jargon-free language that clients demand and courts reward is rooted in the methodologies of plain-language experts like Bryan A. Garner and Richard C. Wydick.1 Before a single word of a privacy policy or terms of service is drafted, the document must be structurally sound, minimizing the cognitive load on the reader through logical sequencing and deliberate formatting.5

### **Structural and Cognitive Engineering**

Documents must be planned for maximal efficiency. Material should be ordered in a logical sequence, utilizing chronology when presenting factual background, and keeping all related material grouped together systematically.5 A fundamental concept in modern drafting is the "deep issue" technique. Legal disclosures should not take multiple pages of preamble to reach the operative point. Documents should utilize the deep issue technique to provide the core premise, restriction, or consent requirement on the first page, if not within the first paragraph.5  
The visual and organizational breakdown of the text is equally critical. Documents must be divided into distinct sections, and those sections broken down into smaller, digestible parts.5 Each section and subsection must utilize an informative heading that accurately describes the content beneath it.5 For instance, a user scanning a website footer link for data deletion protocols should be able to instantly locate the relevant heading without reading the entire document. Furthermore, each paragraph must be introduced with a clear topic sentence, and the text must provide signposts to guide the reader through complex enumerations.5  
The following table outlines the core structural principles required for drafting high-level corporate legal documents, derived from the established plain-language methodologies.5

| Structural Principle | Implementation Strategy | Justification and Cognitive Impact |
| :---- | :---- | :---- |
| **Nonlinear Outlining** | Plan writing projects by grouping related concepts before drafting linear sentences. | Prevents the repetitive looping of concepts that often plagues terms of service agreements.5 |
| **Informative Headings** | Replace vague headings (e.g., "General Provisions") with descriptive markers (e.g., "How to Terminate Your Account"). | Facilitates scanning and drastically reduces the time users spend searching for specific rights or obligations.5 |
| **Topic Sentences** | Ensure the first sentence of every paragraph states the main idea. | Anchors the reader and provides immediate context for the subsequent supporting details.5 |
| **Signposting** | Use clear transitional phrases and structural markers to bridge paragraphs. | Guides the reader smoothly through complex legal logic without losing the narrative thread.5 |
| **Deep Issue Framing** | Spill the crucial details immediately. Define the core agreement upfront. | Prevents users from abandoning the document before understanding their fundamental legal obligations.5 |

### **Syntactic Economy and Sentence Mechanics**

The mechanics of sentence construction directly dictate readability. The analysis indicates that adherence to strict syntactic boundaries drastically improves user comprehension, particularly in environments where users may be reading on mobile devices.3  
The average sentence length must be kept to approximately 20 words.5 While some sentences may be longer or shorter to provide a varied, natural rhythm, the overall average must remain low to accommodate readers with varying levels of literacy.4 A primary cause of sentence bloat is the separation of related words. The subject, the verb, and the object must be kept together and placed toward the beginning of the sentence.5 Interrupting the core action of a sentence with lengthy, parenthetical qualifying clauses creates severe cognitive friction. It forces the reader to hold too much disjointed information in their working memory, inevitably requiring them to reread the text to understand who is doing what to whom.5  
The active voice must always be preferred over the passive voice.5 The formula "Subject \+ Verb \+ Object" clarifies liability and responsibility, which is the paramount goal of any contract. For example, "The user must pay the subscription fee" is vastly superior to "The subscription fee must be paid," as the latter fails to identify the specific actor obligated to perform the duty.9 When a contract is drafted passively, it creates interpretive loopholes regarding who bears the burden of compliance.  
Sentences should end emphatically. The drafter should place the most important piece of information at the close of the thought to ensure it resonates with the reader.5 Furthermore, positive phrasing must be prioritized, and multiple negatives must be avoided at all costs. Instead of stating, "Notice will not be effective unless it is delivered," the text should affirmatively state, "Notice is effective only if delivered".5  
The following table summarizes the key syntactic rules for drafting clear legal prose.5

| Syntactic Rule | Example of Poor Drafting | Plain Language Correction |
| :---- | :---- | :---- |
| **Active Voice** | "Utilities must be paid on the first day of each month." | "The user must pay utilities on the first day of each month." 9 |
| **Subject-Verb Proximity** | "The Company, during the course of its struggle to provide services, accrued debt." | "The Company accrued debt while struggling to provide services." 5 |
| **Positive Phrasing** | "No termination will be approved unless the administrator reviews the application." | "The administrator must review the application to approve a termination." 5 |
| **Emphatic Endings** | "The right to stop the work is the most important factor in this case." | "In this case, the most important factor is the right to stop the work." 5 |

## **Eradicating Legalese: A Lexical Framework**

The most visible element of a legal tone of voice is its vocabulary. The presence of archaic "lawyerisms" gives writing a "legal smell" but carries little to no actual legal substance.1 These words must be systematically banished from the corporate lexicon.1 True legal terms of art—such as plaintiff, defendant, mandamus, or mens rea—are sometimes unavoidable and acceptable, provided they are explained if the context requires it.1 However, "legaldegook" must be excised entirely.1

### **The Banishment Dictionary**

Drafters must ruthlessly edit to omit needless words and archaic phrases that create artificial distance between the brand and the user.1 The following table outlines the archaic terms that must be removed from all drafted documents, alongside their modern, plain-language equivalents and the rationale for their removal.1

| Archaic Terminology (Banish) | Defect and Analytical Rationale | Modern Plain-Language Equivalent |
| :---- | :---- | :---- |
| *Such*, *Said* | Often used incorrectly as adjectives (e.g., "said property"). This archaic usage sounds stuffy, stilted, and introduces unnecessary ambiguity regarding whether it refers to a specific item or a general class.1 | *The*, *this*, *that*, *these*, *those*.1 |
| *As such* | Frequently misused to mean "therefore" or "hence." It strictly requires an antecedent noun.1 | *Therefore*, *hence*, *accordingly*.1 |
| *Pursuant to* | Described as the dangerous "hallmark of legalese." It is highly prevalent in mediocre writing but virtually absent in masterly legal writing.1 | *Under*, *according to*, *in accordance with*.1 |
| *Heretofore* | Carries no legal substance and gives the writing a musty, tired smell.1 | *Before now*, *previously*.1 |
| *Aforementioned* / *Aforesaid* | The useless "big brother" of "said." It is imprecise and confusing when multiple items have been discussed previously in the text.1 | Describe the specific item precisely by name, or simply use *earlier*.1 |
| *Hereinafter called* | A completely unnecessary parenthetical signaling device that disrupts the flow of the introductory clause.1 | Simply state the entity's name and refer to the shorthand moving forward without quotes or parentheses.1 |
| *Witnesseth*, *To wit* | Relics of historical drafting practices with absolutely no modern utility or enforceability benefit.1 | Omit entirely.1 |

### **Advanced Lexical Editing Guidelines**

Beyond simple word replacement, the drafter must address deeper lexical habits that plague the legal profession. First, drafters must avoid doublets and triplets. The legal profession has a historical habit of pairing synonyms (e.g., "null and void," "cease and desist," "give, devise, and bequeath"). These must be avoided; a single, precise word is sufficient and reduces document bloat.5  
Second, drafters must actively search for and destroy nominalizations—nouns masquerading as verbs. Phrases like "give consideration to" must be reduced to "consider." "Obtain verification of" becomes "verify," "make an agreement" becomes "agree," and "remit payment" becomes "pay".9 Turning nominalizations back into strong, precise verbs significantly enhances clarity and drives the narrative energy of the contract.9  
Third, the word "shall" must be meticulously avoided. The word is notoriously ambiguous in contract law, often confused by drafters and courts alike between meaning "must" (a duty), "may" (permission), or "will" (a future event). The most legally rigorous practice is to delete every instance of "shall" and replace it with "must" for an obligation, "will" for a future event, or "may" for permission.5  
Finally, the treatment of definitions requires careful consideration. If definitions are strictly required, they should be defined in plain language at the very beginning of the document using a conversational tone.10 However, if a document requires more than just a few definitions, they should be placed in a schedule at the end of the document, not at the beginning. Front-loading a massive wall of definitions overwhelms the reader and prevents them from reaching the operative clauses of the agreement.5

## **Typography and Conspicuousness: The Empirical Fallacy of All-Caps**

A critical component of a legal document's tone is its visual presentation. Historically, the legal industry relied heavily on formatting text in all capital letters (all-caps) to ensure that important contract language—such as limitations of liability, warranty disclaimers, and arbitration clauses—was deemed "conspicuous" by the courts.12 This practice was rooted in mid-20th-century typesetting limitations, where typewriters lacked formatting options, and early interpretations of the Uniform Commercial Code (UCC) accepted it as a viable method for drawing attention.12

### **The Perceptual Failure of Capitalized Text**

Recent empirical research decisively dismantles the notion that all-caps formatting improves consumer understanding. A comprehensive study by Yonathan A. Arbel and Andrew Toler analyzed standard form contracts from 500 large consumer companies (including Amazon and Uber) and conducted extensive perceptual testing on consumers.14 By utilizing specially developed code to count capitalization instances, the authors revealed profound flaws in the persistent use of all-caps:  
The study demonstrates with high statistical significance that all-caps fails entirely to enhance or improve the quality of consumer consent, which is defined as the ability to recall and understand terms.14 Standard lowercase text (low-caps) was proven to be no worse than all-caps in terms of comprehension.14 More alarmingly, capitalization actively diminishes the quality of consent for older demographics. In testing a cancellation policy for a free trial, 60% of respondents older than 55 years of age answered questions incorrectly when the policy was presented in all-caps, compared to only 31% in the low-caps control group.14  
All-caps does not increase the salience of the text, nor does it successfully focus reader attention. Instead, it decreases readability. The authors hypothesize that all-caps acts as "textual yelling," which creates significant cognitive friction and actively discourages user engagement.14 Because approximately 77% of standard consumer contracts contain at least one fully capitalized paragraph, consumers have fallen into a "habituation trap." They have become habituated to ignoring these blocks of text entirely.14  
For a healthcare website—which inherently serves a vast demographic encompassing elderly and visually impaired users—relying on all-caps to convey critical health privacy disclosures is actively detrimental to the user experience. It weakens the empirical basis for informed consent and directly contradicts plain language accessibility goals.14 The legal faith that judges and legislators place in capitalized clauses is entirely unsupported by empirical evidence and is based on mere speculation and outdated intuition.14

### **Modern Standards for Digital Conspicuousness**

Under modern legal interpretation, conspicuousness is a performance standard, not merely a font size or a capitalization rule.16 The Federal Trade Commission (FTC) guidelines for digital disclosures dictate that a disclosure is only clear and conspicuous if consumers actually notice it, read it, and understand it.16 The combination of long, legalistic text and all-capital letters is highly unlikely to be considered clear in a modern consumer context.17  
To achieve true conspicuousness without relying on the ineffective all-caps crutch, drafters must utilize a creative mix of visual hierarchy, much like marketing professionals do when they actually want to communicate effectively with consumers.14 The following table outlines the modern visual elements required for drafting conspicuous online agreements.

| Visual Element | Application Rule and Justification | Cautions and Limitations |
| :---- | :---- | :---- |
| **Bolding** | Use sparingly to highlight specific key terms, deadlines, or critical sentences. Empirical testing shows bolding can enhance comprehension.14 | Avoid the habituation trap. If a contract makes everything bold, it becomes impossible for a consumer to differentiate important terms from unimportant ones.14 |
| **Boxes and Borders** | Place highly critical limitations of liability or opt-out notices inside a distinct visual box.13 | Draws the eye naturally without altering the readability of the standard text inside the box. Must not be overused. |
| **White Space** | Create ample white space and use it meaningfully between paragraphs and distinct clauses.5 | Prevents visual fatigue and makes the document appear approachable rather than like an impenetrable, intimidating wall of text.5 |
| **Standard Sentence Case** | Use standard capitalization rules for all body text, regardless of its underlying legal importance.14 | Dramatically improves reading speed and cognitive comprehension, especially for visually impaired or elderly users.14 |
| **Bullets** | Highlight related ideas or lists with attention-getters such as bullets, rather than embedding them in long, comma-separated sentences.5 | Breaks down enumerations into parallel provisions that are easy for the user to scan rapidly.5 |

## **Integrating Brand Tone with Legal Rigor**

A corporate legal style guide must function as a bridge between strict legal compliance and the organization's overarching brand identity. This requires a dedicated UX (User Experience) content strategy applied directly to legal texts.18 The tone of voice for legal documents should generally be defined as "professional but approachable" or "authoritative and neutral".7 While legal content deals with serious, binding matters and requires a slightly more formal tone than marketing copy, it must remain inherently accessible.10  
Leading technology and finance companies have successfully demonstrated how to humanize legal text without sacrificing a shred of enforceability.10 A consistent tone builds trust, making the product feel more polished and reliable to the end-user.18 The following principles must be integrated into the organization's legal tone of voice to achieve this balance.  
First, the utilization of contractions (e.g., "we're," "it's," "don't") is highly encouraged. Using contractions makes the writing sound more human and flow naturally with the rest of the brand voice.6 Contracting words does not affect the legal validity or binding nature of an agreement.10 Mixing contractions with spelled-out words creates a standard, conversational rhythm that sits comfortably between overly formal and excessively casual.6  
Second, documents should utilize friendly pronouns. Instead of referring to the parties as "The Corporation" and "The User" repetitively throughout the text, the document should define the parties upfront using standard pronouns. For example: *"This agreement is between the Company ('we,' 'us,' or 'our') and you, the user ('you' or 'your')."* After defining them, writers are free to use these friendly pronouns throughout the remainder of the text, instantly reducing the intimidation factor of the document.10  
Third, drafters should prioritize contextual explanations over separate plain-language summaries. When a legal term of art is absolutely unavoidable due to strict case law precedent (e.g., "represent and warrant"), the drafter should provide a quick definition or real-world example inline to help the user understand.10 Some companies provide a "plain language summary" column alongside the dense legal text; however, the superior approach is to simply write the operative text in plain language to begin with. If more context is needed, sidebars or hyperlinks should be used to provide examples or further reading, rather than maintaining two distinct versions of the text.10  
Finally, business buzzwords must be rigorously avoided. Terms like "synergy," "bandwidth," or "leverage" alienate readers, irritate legal professionals, and fundamentally lack legal precision.6 Furthermore, colloquialisms and regional sayings should be avoided entirely, as they translate poorly and may severely confuse non-native speakers or international users engaging with the platform.6

## **Jurisdictional Compliance: The California Baseline**

Because the default jurisdiction for this tone of voice framework includes California, the drafting standards must satisfy some of the strictest and most granular consumer protection and privacy laws in the United States. Adhering to California standards inherently establishes a high baseline of compliance that satisfies most other state requirements, protecting the organization from multi-state litigation vectors.

### **The California Consumer Privacy Act (CCPA) and CPRA**

The CCPA, as amended by the California Privacy Rights Act (CPRA), fundamentally reshapes how privacy notices and terms must be written.21 The law expressly targets corporate obfuscation and mandates transparency regarding data collection, sharing, and user rights.  
According to CCPA Regulations § 999.305(a)(2), any "notice at collection" or overarching privacy policy must meet strict formatting and linguistic criteria.3 The notice must use "plain, straightforward language" and strictly avoid the use of "technical or legal jargon".3 It must be formatted in a way that actively draws the consumer's attention and must be highly readable, specifically noting that it must remain readable when viewed on smaller mobile screens.3 Furthermore, the notice must be reasonably accessible to consumers with disabilities, aligning with broader digital accessibility standards.3  
A critical component of California privacy law involves the use of Automated Decision-Making Technology (ADMT). If a business utilizes ADMT, the pre-use notice provided to the consumer must include a plain language explanation of exactly how the ADMT works, the specific purpose of its use, and a clear, conspicuous description of the consumer's right to opt-out.23 Links facilitating these opt-outs, such as "Do Not Sell or Share My Personal Information," must be clearly labeled, conspicuous, and completely jargon-free.24

### **The California Government Code and Readability Standards**

California's commitment to plain language extends beyond privacy regulations into broad statutory requirements. California Government Code 6219 requires state agencies to write every document they produce in plain, straightforward language, avoiding technical terms as much as possible, and utilizing a coherent and easily readable style.4  
While this specific statute governs state agencies, the standard established by the Office of Data and Innovation (ODI) sets the benchmark for all consumer-facing public documents within the state.4 The ODI's statewide plain language equity standard mandates that texts be tested for readability to ensure they meet a **Grade 8 reading level or below**.4 Documents must use common words, limit sentence length to a maximum of 20 words, and define any abbreviations or acronyms upon their first use (e.g., spelling out Federal Bureau of Investigation before using FBI).4 Adopting this Grade 8 reading level standard for corporate website footers ensures that the documents remain heavily insulated against claims of deceptive complexity in consumer protection litigation.

### **Consumer Contract Awareness and Translation Requirements**

The Plain Language Consumer Contract Act (PLCCA) and the Consumer Contract Awareness Act of 1990 (Civil Code 1799.201) are designed to ensure that consumer contracts—writings prepared by a seller and signed by a consumer for personal, family, or household purposes—are written in clear and straightforward language.26 These acts aim to protect consumers by ensuring they can easily comprehend their rights and obligations, fostering a more informed marketplace.27  
Furthermore, California Civil Code Section 1632 (The California Translation Act) serves to protect the state's highly diverse population. If a person engaged in a trade or business negotiates primarily in Spanish, Chinese, Tagalog, Vietnamese, or Korean (the five most commonly spoken non-English languages in the state), they must deliver a translation of every term and condition of the consumer contract to the other party prior to the execution of the agreement.30  
Recent legislative amendments (AB 3254\) have expanded these protections significantly. The translation requirements have been extended to non-party signatories, meaning the right to a translated contract is now available to any person signing the contract, including guarantors and co-signers.33 If a website conducts business, marketing, or customer support functions in any of these five languages, the underlying legal documents located in the footer must be expertly translated into those respective languages to avoid the risk of a consumer rescinding the contract due to translation deficiencies.3  
The following table summarizes the primary California regulatory baseline requirements that must be integrated into the drafting protocol.3

| California Regulation | Core Drafting Requirement | Operational Impact on Footer Documents |
| :---- | :---- | :---- |
| **CCPA / CPRA** | "Plain, straightforward language" free of technical jargon.3 | Opt-out links and data collection notices must be instantly understandable and mobile-friendly.3 |
| **Gov. Code 6219 (ODI Standard)** | Readability testing resulting in a Grade 8 reading level or below.4 | Requires strict adherence to the 20-word sentence limit and basic vocabulary choices.4 |
| **Civil Code 1632 (Translation Act)** | Translated contracts for Spanish, Chinese, Tagalog, Vietnamese, or Korean.31 | If the site markets in these languages, footer links must route to corresponding, fully translated legal policies.31 |

## **Industry-Specific Compliance: Healthcare and Federal Regulations**

For businesses operating in the healthcare sector, legal disclosures are heavily scrutinized by federal regulators, particularly the Federal Trade Commission (FTC) and the Department of Health and Human Services (HHS). The tone of voice must convey authority, security, and absolute transparency regarding the handling of sensitive Protected Health Information (PHI).

### **HIPAA Authorization Standards**

Under the Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act (HIPAA), organizations (covered entities and business associates) must obtain valid authorization before using or disclosing consumer health information for commercial activities or reasons outside of standard treatment, payment, or operations.35  
The HHS explicitly dictates that a valid HIPAA authorization **must be written in plain language**.15 The regulatory guidance is unambiguous: if the individual cannot understand the authorization, the authorization is legally ineffective, rendering the subsequent data collection or sharing unlawful and subject to severe penalties.37  
A compliant, plain-language HIPAA authorization must clearly and succinctly explain the "who, what, when, where, and why" of the data transaction 36:

* **Who:** Exactly who is authorized to make the disclosure, and who is receiving the information.38  
* **What:** The precise nature and description of the health information being shared.38  
* **When:** The specific date, event, or condition upon which the permission to disclose expires.38  
* **Where/With Whom:** The specific entities or categories of entities the data will be shared with.38  
* **Why:** The specific purpose for which the information will be used and disclosed.15

### **The FTC Act and Health Breach Notification Rule**

Healthcare applications, fitness trackers, diet applications, and connected devices that collect health data but do not strictly fall under the purview of HIPAA are still heavily regulated by the Federal Trade Commission.39 Section 5 of the FTC Act prohibits deceptive or unfair business practices, which inherently includes the practice of burying critical data-sharing practices in incomprehensible, lengthy terms of service.39 If a healthcare website makes a privacy promise—either expressly or by implication—it must live up to it, and those promises must be stated unequivocally in simple language.40  
Furthermore, the FTC's Health Breach Notification Rule applies directly to vendors of personal health records (PHRs), PHR-related entities, and third-party service providers.39 Recent July 2024 amendments to the rule make it explicitly clear that makers of health apps and connected devices must comply.39 Disclosures regarding security practices, data handling, and breach notification protocols must be easily located and understood by the everyday user, maintaining the FTC's performance standard for clear and conspicuous communication.16

### **Medical Disclaimers**

A specialized requirement for healthcare website footers is the Medical Disclaimer. This text declares that the content provided on the site is for informational purposes only and should never be considered professional medical advice, nor should it serve as a substitute for consulting a licensed healthcare provider.42  
If a website offers any advice or content that sounds medical in nature, posting a medical disclaimer is critical to help limit legal liabilities if a user experiences harm while following the information on the website.42 Like all other clauses discussed in this framework, this disclaimer must avoid the use of excessive capitalization and instead rely on clear, concise language, formatted properly within the website footer, to effectively limit liability.42

## **Future-Proofing for Global Accessibility (EU Considerations)**

While the default jurisdiction for these documents is the United States and California, the architecture of the legal text must be high-level enough to avoid hindering future compliance with European Union regulations. The intersection of plain language and digital accessibility is currently being codified into strict law across the EU, representing the global gold standard for digital legal text and user inclusivity.

### **The European Accessibility Act (EAA)**

The European Accessibility Act (Directive 2019/882), which became enforceable across all EU Member States on June 28, 2025, mandates that digital products, services, and electronic documents (including PDFs, e-commerce platforms, consumer banking contracts, and transport services) be fully accessible to people with disabilities.45 This applies to any company operating within the EU market or selling services to EU consumers, regardless of where the company is headquartered geographically.46 Non-compliance can lead to severe fines or market bans.47  
Under the EAA, product documentation, contracts, and legal disclosures must adhere to strict formatting, linguistic, and sensory rules.48 The complexity of the language used in consumer-facing contracts and information must be actively simplified so that it does not exceed a **B2 language proficiency level** (upper intermediate).48  
Furthermore, documents must align with the four core principles of accessibility: they must be Perceivable, Operable, Understandable, and Robust.45 A contract written in complex legalese fundamentally fails the "Understandable" criterion, rendering it non-compliant under the EAA, even if the underlying digital interface is accessible.48 Information must also be presented through multiple sensory channels (e.g., text paired with video or auditory explanations), and the text formatting must allow for the seamless generation of alternative assistive formats like screen readers.48 This requires sufficient color contrast, proper HTML/ARIA coding, appropriately sized fonts, and descriptive alt-text for any images contained within the documentation.45

### **General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR) Alignment**

Drafting in plain English natively prepares the organization for GDPR compliance regarding privacy policies and notices. Transparency and informing the public about how their data is being used are basic, foundational goals of the GDPR.49  
Article 12 of the GDPR explicitly requires that privacy notices and any communications relating to data processing be delivered in a "concise, transparent, intelligible, and easily accessible form, using clear and plain language".49 This is particularly heavily enforced for any information addressed specifically to a child.49 Vague qualifiers such as "we may," "we might," "some," or "often" are strictly prohibited under European Commission guidelines; statements must use the active voice and clearly articulate precise actions regarding data processing.49  
Articles 13 and 14 dictate the precise information that must be provided depending on whether the data is collected directly from the subject (First-Party) or obtained indirectly (Third-Party).49 This includes detailing the legal basis for processing, retention periods, data transfer safeguards, and the existence of automated decision-making systems.49 To give an easily visible and intelligible overview of intended processing, the GDPR even encourages presenting this information in combination with standardized, machine-readable icons.49  
By adopting the plain language standards dictated by California's CCPA and Government Code 6219, the organization organically positions its documents to meet the stringent intelligibility requirements of both the GDPR and the EAA, ensuring global compatibility without requiring a complete structural rewrite during international expansion.4

## **Synthesis: Drafting and Review Protocol for Website Footer Disclosures**

To operationalize this extensive research, the drafting of actual website footer documents (e.g., Privacy Policy, Terms of Service, Medical Disclaimer) must follow a rigorous, standardized process that attorneys can easily replicate.51

### **Required Elements for Website Terms and Policies**

When drafting the Terms of Service and Privacy Policy, the following clauses should be logically organized, utilizing informative headings, and written in Grade 8 plain English 51:

1. **Introduction and Acceptance of Terms:** A clear, active-voice statement explaining that using the service constitutes agreement to the terms, detailing how users are notified of updates.54  
2. **User Eligibility and Responsibilities:** Plain language outlining age restrictions, account creation requirements, password protection duties, and acceptable use guidelines.54  
3. **Data Collection and Privacy Practices:** Identity of the data controller, specific categories of data collected, the precise purpose of collection, data sharing practices, and user rights (deletion, access, opt-out).22  
4. **Intellectual Property and User Content:** Rules regarding copyright retention and clear DMCA takedown notice procedures.51  
5. **Disclaimers and Limitations of Liability:** Warranties, medical disclaimers, and liability limits formatted using bolding or bordered boxes for conspicuousness, strictly avoiding the use of all-caps.14  
6. **Dispute Resolution and Governing Law:** How disputes will be handled (e.g., arbitration parameters) and the applicable legal jurisdiction.51  
7. **Contact Information:** Clear, accessible pathways for users to reach out with privacy requests or legal concerns.52

### **The Editing and Testing Protocol**

A legal document is not complete once drafted; it must undergo systematic editing and readability testing to ensure it meets both statutory requirements and corporate tone guidelines.4

* **The Hemingway or Readability Test:** Text must be run through readability software (such as the Hemingway Editor) to verify that it meets the Grade 8 reading level standard mandated by California equity standards.4 Because proper nouns and industry-specific terms (like HIPAA or ADMT) may artificially inflate the score, the text should be tested both with and without these terms to accurately gauge the structural complexity of the sentences.4  
* **The Sibling/Layperson Test:** As recommended by federal plain language guidelines, the drafter should test their assumptions by having a non-lawyer read the text prior to publication. If a layperson cannot summarize the core obligations of the text, it fails the performance standard for clear and conspicuous disclosure.3  
* **Web Standard Formatting:** The final text must be designed explicitly for screen reading. It should utilize working hyperlinks with clear, conspicuous labels.56 The web layout must be clean, responsive to smaller mobile screens, and compatible with WCAG 2.1 and EAA assistive technologies to ensure universal digital accessibility.3

## **Conclusion**

The evolution of legal document drafting is unequivocally clear: enforceability is no longer achieved through impenetrable linguistic density, but through absolute transparency and cognitive accessibility. For a highly regulated entity, particularly operating within the intersection of technology and healthcare, the adoption of a plain language tone of voice is not merely a stylistic preference; it is a strategic imperative. It fulfills federal FTC mandates regarding conspicuous disclosures, satisfies strict California privacy and readability regulations, and anticipates emerging global digital accessibility standards like the EAA and GDPR.  
By adhering strictly to the principles of structural engineering, syntactic economy, and modern typography—specifically focusing on the eradication of archaic lawyerisms and the total abandonment of capitalized text—legal documents transition from defensive, user-hostile liabilities into strategic assets that actively build consumer trust. This comprehensive framework ensures that every clickable link in the organization's website footer delivers legal information that is precise, fully compliant, and undeniably human.

# Legal Tone of Voice Standard
### A General-Purpose Style Guide for Legal Documents
*Prepared for Pixelday · June 2026 · Version 1.0*

***

## Purpose and Scope

This document establishes the tone, voice, and writing standards for all legal documents produced at Pixelday, drafted in collaboration with licensed US attorneys who review and execute each document. It is designed as a **general-purpose standard** applicable across industries and document types — from healthcare website disclosures to commercial contracts and beyond. While grounded in US law, particularly California requirements, this guide is written to be forward-compatible with EU regulatory frameworks (notably GDPR) without making EU compliance its primary mandate.

The primary audience for Pixelday legal documents is a **mixed readership of everyday users and legal professionals**. This standard explicitly rejects the practice of intentional obfuscation through legalese. As Richard C. Wydick's foundational text *Plain English for Lawyers* states, "Good legal writing should not differ, without good reason, from ordinary, well-written English." Accessible legal language is not a compromise of legal precision — it is its highest expression.[1][2]

***

## Governing Principles

### 1. Clarity Over Cleverness
Every document must be understandable the first time a reader encounters it. The Plain Language Action and Information Network (PLAIN) defines plain language as "communication your audience can understand the first time they read or hear it." This is the controlling standard. If a sentence requires re-reading to be understood, it must be rewritten.[3]

### 2. Precision Without Obfuscation
Legal terms of art that carry specific, irreplaceable legal meaning should be retained (e.g., *indemnification*, *arbitration*, *plaintiff*, *PHI*). Archaic filler language with no legal function must be eliminated. The San Francisco Bar Association specifically lists the following as language to eliminate from legal writing: *heretofore*, *hereinafter referred to as*, *witnesseth*, *thereto*, *henceforth*, and similarly outdated constructions. True terms of art are not the same as legaldegook — and the difference matters.[1]

### 3. Empowerment, Not Subordination
Legal documents should empower readers to understand their rights and obligations, not leave them dependent on an attorney to translate the document for them. As Clarity International notes, "A legal document written in plain language empowers readers. Legislation written in plain language requires no translation for laypeople to understand." This principle is especially critical for disclosures that appear on public-facing websites.[2]

### 4. Regulatory Alignment as a Floor, Not a Ceiling
This guide is designed to meet — and where possible, meaningfully exceed — the minimum plain language requirements set by US federal law, California state law, and HIPAA. The Plain Writing Act of 2010 requires federal agencies to write "clear Government communication that the public can understand and use." HIPAA's Notice of Privacy Practices rule requires that covered entities "write its notice in plain language," achievable by "writing short sentences in the active voice, using 'you' and other pronouns where appropriate, and using common, everyday words." The CCPA requires privacy policies to be "written in plain English and formatted in readable text that's easy to navigate." GDPR Article 12 requires disclosures in "concise, transparent, intelligible and easily accessible form, using clear and plain language." This guide satisfies all four frameworks simultaneously.[4][5][6][3]

***

## Voice Attributes

The Pixelday legal voice can be summarized in four paired attributes:

| Attribute | What It Means | What It Is Not |
|-----------|--------------|----------------|
| **Clear** | Sentences are direct and immediately understood | Vague, euphemistic, or requiring inference |
| **Honest** | All material terms are disclosed upfront, not buried | Deceptive, misleading, or strategically obscure |
| **Respectful** | Treats all readers — regardless of legal literacy — as intelligent adults | Condescending or needlessly technical |
| **Precise** | Uses exact language that cannot be misread or exploited | Ambiguous, hedged without cause, or over-qualified |

## Tone Calibration by Document Type

Different legal documents sit on a formality spectrum. The following guidance applies across the most common document types encountered in web-based deployments:

| Document Type | Tone Register | Reader Expectation |
|---------------|--------------|-------------------|
| Privacy Policy | Informative, calm, direct | "What do you collect and why?" |
| Terms of Service / Terms of Use | Firm, fair, clear | "What are the rules of engagement?" |
| Accessibility Statement | Inviting, committed | "Is this site usable for me?" |
| Notice of Privacy Practices (HIPAA) | Clinical, plain, respectful | "What happens to my health data?" |
| Cookie Policy | Casual-adjacent, brief | "What are you tracking?" |
| Disclaimer / Limitation of Liability | Direct, unambiguous | "What are you responsible for?" |
| Arbitration / Dispute Resolution Clause | Neutral, fair, explanatory | "What happens if something goes wrong?" |

Tone does not change the legal substance of any document. It changes how clearly that substance is communicated. A well-calibrated tone signals the organization's values without sacrificing enforceability.

## Grammar and Style Rules

### Active Voice
Active voice is the default for all legal documents produced under this standard. Active voice makes clear who is doing what, reducing ambiguity that can create legal exposure. Passive voice should be used only when the actor is unknown, genuinely irrelevant, or when active construction creates an awkward sentence. Target: no more than 5% passive sentences in any document.[7][8]

> ✅ **Active:** "We collect your email address to send you appointment reminders."
> ❌ **Passive:** "Your email address is collected for the purpose of appointment reminder delivery."

### Sentence Length
Target an average sentence length of **15–20 words**. The State Bar of Michigan notes that typical legal contracts average 42 words per sentence — a figure that is "well, huge" compared to the 15–20 word target for readable everyday English. Sentences above 30 words should be broken into two unless the content is inseparably compound.[9][10][11]

### Readability Target
All public-facing legal documents should aim for a **Flesch Reading Ease score of 50–65** (roughly plain-language professional standard) and a **Flesch-Kincaid Grade Level of 9–12**. This range is appropriate for a mixed audience of consumers and professionals. Documents targeting general consumers alone should aim for Grade Level 8 or below where legally feasible. Microsoft Word's built-in Readability Statistics tool can measure both scores during the drafting process.[12][13]

### Person and Pronouns
Use **first and second person** throughout. Third person creates distance and signals that a document is written for someone else. Second-person engagement makes legal obligations and rights feel personal and actionable.[9]

> ✅ "You have the right to request deletion of your data."
> ❌ "Users may request deletion of personal information."

Use "we" and "our" for the organization. Use "you" and "your" for the user/consumer/patient. Avoid "the user," "the consumer," or "the undersigned" unless the document is a formal contract requiring specific identification of parties.

### Nominalization
Avoid nominalizations — abstract nouns formed from verbs — as they make sentences dense and passive. Nominalization adds syllables and cognitive load without adding precision.[14]

> ✅ "We provide your data to our service partners."
> ❌ "Provision of your data to service partners is conducted by us."

### Compound Phrases
Replace multi-word compound constructions with single words wherever meaning is preserved.[11]

| Instead of... | Use... |
|---------------|--------|
| "in order to" | "to" |
| "for the purpose of" | "to" or "for" |
| "make utilization of" | "use" |
| "in the event that" | "if" |
| "notwithstanding the foregoing" | "Despite the above" or restructure |
| "pursuant to" | "under" or "as required by" |

### Defined Terms
When a term needs to be defined for legal clarity, define it naturally in the first use, then use the defined term consistently throughout. Avoid the archaic practice of placing defined names in quotation marks inside parentheses after the full name of an entity.[1]

> ✅ "Pixelday Health, Inc. (Pixelday) collects..."
> ❌ "Pixelday Health, Inc. (hereinafter referred to as "Company") collects..."

## Structural Standards

### Lead with What Matters
Front-load documents and sections with the key information readers need most. Summaries at the top of sections — particularly in privacy policies and terms of service — let readers find what they need without reading every word. Detail follows the summary, not the reverse.[14]

### Headings and Navigation
Use clear, descriptive headings that tell readers what a section covers before they read it. Headings should be written in plain English, not legalese.[15][16]

> ✅ "How We Use Your Information"
> ❌ "Collection, Use, and Disclosure of Personal Data Pursuant to Applicable Regulations"

### Lists and Bullets
Use bulleted or numbered lists for any enumerated set of three or more items. Dense paragraph structures embedding multiple rights, obligations, or conditions are harder to read and more likely to be overlooked — including by courts reviewing enforceability.[9]

### One Idea Per Sentence; One Topic Per Section
A sentence should carry one logical unit. A section should address one topic. If a section is addressing two legally distinct issues, split it.[11]

### Document-Level Architecture
For public-facing website legal documents, the recommended structure is:

1. **Plain-language summary** (2–5 sentences, top of document)
2. **Effective date and last updated date** — prominently displayed[17]
3. **Numbered or headed sections** for each substantive topic
4. **Consumer rights and how to exercise them** — clearly identified, not buried[18]
5. **Contact information** — name, email, phone, and physical address[19]
6. **Version history** — for attorney tracking and audit purposes

## Words and Phrases to Avoid

The following terms should be replaced or eliminated unless they are unavoidable terms of art in a specific legal context:

| Avoid | Replace With |
|-------|-------------|
| Hereinafter | [the defined term directly] |
| Witnesseth | [omit entirely] |
| Heretofore | "Before now" or "Previously" |
| Hereto / Herein / Thereof | Rewrite the sentence using explicit nouns |
| Pursuant to | "Under" or "As required by" |
| Notwithstanding | "Despite" or "Even if" |
| Forthwith | "Immediately" |
| Inter alia | "Including" or "Among other things" |
| Said [noun] | "[The noun]" |
| The undersigned | "[Party name]" or "You" |
| Shall | "Will" (for obligations); "May" (for permissions)[20] |
| Witnesseth | [omit] |
| Please find enclosed / Attached hereto | "Attached is" or "We've included" |
| Kindly be advised | [omit; lead directly with the information] |

**Note on "shall" vs. "will":** Modern plain language legal drafting increasingly favors "will" for obligations and "may" for permissions, as "shall" creates interpretive ambiguity and reads as archaic to lay audiences. This substitution is consistent with modern US contract drafting norms and should be applied in all consumer-facing documents.[20]

## Accessibility Integration

Legal documents displayed on websites are subject to the same accessibility standards as all other digital content. As of April 24, 2026, healthcare providers receiving Medicare, Medicaid, or CHIP funding must meet **WCAG 2.1 Level AA** standards across all web content. Plain language and accessibility are mutually reinforcing:[27][28]

- **Plain language reduces cognitive load**, meeting WCAG's "Understandable" principle[31]
- **Proper heading structure** in legal documents enables screen reader navigation[32]
- **Sufficient color contrast** (minimum 4.5:1 ratio) must be applied to all legal document text rendered on web pages[32]
- **Footer links** to legal documents must use descriptive, plain-English link text (e.g., "Privacy Policy" — not "Legal" or "Click here")[33][34]
- **PDFs** of legal documents must be tagged and structured for assistive technology compatibility[35]

***

## Footer-Specific Guidance

Legal links displayed in website footers are a high-stakes UX and compliance surface. The following applies to footer-delivered legal documents across all industries:

- **Link text must match document title exactly** — what users click must match what they find
- **All required documents must be visible** on every page via the footer — not just the homepage[33]
- **Do not bury disclosures** — the FTC holds that disclosures placed only in footers, with small text or low contrast, may not meet the "clear and conspicuous" standard[26]
- **Recommended plain-English footer link labels** for healthcare websites:
  - Privacy Policy
  - Notice of Privacy Practices
  - Terms of Use
  - Accessibility Statement
  - Cookie Policy
  - Do Not Sell or Share My Personal Information *(required exact language under CCPA)*[17]
  - Non-Discrimination Notice *(where applicable)*

Under the CCPA 2026 amendments, the "Do Not Sell or Share My Personal Information" link must use this exact phrasing or a "reasonably similar" variant, and the homepage must confirm when a GPC (Global Privacy Control) opt-out signal has been honored — for example, displaying "Opt-Out Request Honored" persistently in the footer.[36][22]

## EU Forward Compatibility Notes

While this document is US-first, the following practices ensure it remains compatible with GDPR-governed documents without modification:

- **GDPR requires the same plain language standard** as CCPA — both demand clear, accessible, intelligible disclosures[29][3]
- **First/second person usage** ("we" / "you") satisfies both CCPA and GDPR transparency principles
- **Data retention periods disclosed by category** is required under 2026 CCPA regulations and strongly recommended under GDPR[22][17]
- **Specific third-party recipient categories** must be named — both CCPA and GDPR prohibit vague descriptions like "business partners"[17]
- **Children's data** requires plain language "that the child can easily understand" under GDPR; the CCPA 2026 amendments automatically classify data from anyone under 16 as sensitive[29][22]

When drafting documents that may be read by EU residents, apply this standard as written. The key EU-specific additions that would be needed (legal basis for processing, DPO contact details, cross-border transfer safeguards) can be added as discrete sections without altering the tone or style established here.

## Do / Don't Quick Reference

### Document Opening
> ✅ **Do:** "This Privacy Policy explains what information we collect about you, how we use it, and the choices you have."
> ❌ **Don't:** "This Privacy Policy (hereinafter 'Policy') sets forth the terms and conditions under which [Company Name] (hereinafter 'Company') collects, uses, and discloses personally identifiable information..."

### Rights Language
> ✅ **Do:** "You have the right to ask us to delete your personal information. To make a request, email us at [email] or call [number]."
> ❌ **Don't:** "Consumers may, pursuant to applicable law, submit a verifiable consumer request for the deletion of personal information collected by the business, subject to applicable exceptions."

### Limitation of Liability
> ✅ **Do:** "To the extent permitted by law, our liability is limited to the amount you paid us in the 12 months before the claim arose."
> ❌ **Don't:** "Notwithstanding the foregoing, in no event shall Company be liable for any indirect, incidental, special, exemplary, consequential, or punitive damages, howsoever caused and regardless of the theory of liability, arising out of or in connection with the use of, or the inability to use, the Services."

### Data Use
> ✅ **Do:** "We share your name and appointment date with your insurance provider to process your claim."
> ❌ **Don't:** "Personal information may be disclosed to third-party service providers for business purposes including but not limited to the processing and administration of claims."

# Legal Tone of Voice

Version: 1.0  
Date: June 7, 2026  
Default jurisdictional posture: United States, California included by default  
Primary audience: licensed attorneys, legal operations, compliance, product, design, and content teams preparing attorney-reviewed legal documents

## Purpose

This document defines the tone, structure, and drafting standards for legal documents that should be clear to everyday readers and credible to legal professionals. It is intended for documents that licensed attorneys will review, revise, approve, and sign off on. It does not replace legal advice, legal review, jurisdictional analysis, or professional judgment.

The standard is intentionally anti-obfuscation. Legal documents should not use complexity as a shield, bury material terms, or signal that the organization expects readers to give up. The voice should communicate that the organization respects the reader’s time, intelligence, rights, and choices.

The core rule is this: write legal documents so the reader can understand what matters, what they can do, what the organization can do, what limits apply, and how to get help. If a sentence is legally accurate but ordinary readers cannot understand its practical effect, the sentence is not finished.

## Foundational position

Plain legal writing is not casual writing, simplified law, or a reduction in legal rigor. The SEC’s plain-English handbook states that plain English does not mean deleting complex information, and that the goal is the orderly and clear presentation of complex information so readers have the best possible chance of understanding it ([SEC Plain English Handbook](https://www.sec.gov/pdf/handbook.pdf)). ISO 24495-1:2023 likewise describes plain language as a discipline for developing printed and digital text, including public documents, technical writing, legislative drafting, and controlled languages ([ISO 24495-1:2023](https://www.iso.org/standard/78907.html)).

Plain legal writing is a precision discipline. It requires the drafter to know the legal effect of a provision well enough to say it directly. The SEC cautions against legalistic or overly complex presentations, vague boilerplate, complex information copied from legal documents without clear explanation, and repetitive disclosure that increases length without improving quality ([SEC Plain English Handbook](https://www.sec.gov/pdf/handbook.pdf)).

For public-facing legal documents, tone and interface are inseparable. FTC online-disclosure guidance says required disclosures must be clear and conspicuous, close to the claim they qualify, prominent, understandable to the intended audience, and not hidden in long terms or hyperlinks when the information is integral to the claim ([FTC Dot Com Disclosures](https://www.ftc.gov/sites/default/files/attachments/press-releases/ftc-staff-revises-online-advertising-disclosure-guidelines/130312dotcomdisclosures.pdf)). California privacy regulations require consumer disclosures and communications to be easy to read and understandable, to use plain and straightforward language, to avoid technical or legal jargon, to be readable on smaller screens, and to be reasonably accessible to consumers with disabilities ([11 CCR § 7003](https://www.law.cornell.edu/regulations/california/11-CCR-7003)).

## Operating standard

A legal document written in this voice should be:

- **Clear**: The reader can understand the practical meaning without rereading several times.
- **Precise**: Rights, obligations, conditions, exceptions, dates, limits, and consequences are stated accurately.
- **Direct**: The sentence names the actor and action whenever the actor matters.
- **Transparent**: Material terms appear where the reader needs them, not only in a remote policy or footer.
- **Calm**: The document is professional, steady, and not threatening unless the legal consequence itself requires a serious tone.
- **Respectful**: The reader is addressed as a person with rights and choices, not as a compliance object.
- **Accessible**: The document is usable on mobile screens, with assistive technologies, and by readers with varying levels of legal literacy.
- **Reviewable**: Attorneys and compliance reviewers can trace the language to legal requirements, business practices, and source materials.

The voice is not cute, casual, promotional, defensive, evasive, punitive, or performatively simple. It should sound like a skilled attorney and a skilled communicator worked together.

## Reader model

Every document should be written for two audiences at once: everyday users and legal professionals. Everyday users need to understand what the document means for them. Legal professionals need to confirm that the document is complete, precise, enforceable where applicable, and consistent with the governing legal framework.

Use the everyday reader as the readability floor and the attorney as the precision standard. Do not make the reader decode legal mechanics just to understand a practical choice. Do not make the attorney infer the intended legal effect from friendly but imprecise wording.

Assume that many readers are busy, mobile, and task-oriented. The FTC advises that advertisers should assume consumers do not read every word of a website or online screen, and that a disclosure not seen or understood cannot correct a misleading impression ([FTC Dot Com Disclosures](https://www.ftc.gov/sites/default/files/attachments/press-releases/ftc-staff-revises-online-advertising-disclosure-guidelines/130312dotcomdisclosures.pdf)).

Assume readers may arrive with stress, urgency, disability, low trust, limited English proficiency, health concerns, financial concerns, or no familiarity with legal vocabulary. In healthcare and other regulated industries, the reader may be making decisions under pressure. The tone should reduce confusion, not add to it.

## Voice principles

### Speak plainly, not loosely

Use the plainest wording that preserves the intended legal effect. A short sentence is not automatically better if it becomes vague. A familiar word is not automatically better if it erases a required legal distinction.

Use ordinary words by default. Use legal terms when the legal term carries necessary meaning, appears in a required statutory phrase, or helps the document align with a defined legal framework. When a legal term is necessary but not familiar, explain it in place.

Example:

| Less effective | Better |
|---|---|
| We may disclose PHI for TPO purposes as permitted by law. | If HIPAA applies, we may use and share protected health information for treatment, payment, and health care operations, as allowed by law. |

### Name who does what

Use “we,” “us,” and “our” for the organization. Use “you” and “your” for the reader. Use the organization’s legal name where the distinction between related entities matters.

The SEC encourages personal pronouns because they reduce abstraction, clarify who must do what, and keep sentences shorter ([SEC Plain English Handbook](https://www.sec.gov/pdf/handbook.pdf)). Use passive voice only when the actor is unknown, legally irrelevant, or intentionally secondary.

Example:

| Less effective | Better |
|---|---|
| Information may be shared with service providers. | We may share information with service providers that help us operate the site. |

### Put the practical point first

State the rule before the exception. State the action before the process. State the consequence before the citation. Give context before detail.

The SEC recommends presenting the big picture before details and using summaries to orient the reader rather than burying the reader in copied operative text ([SEC Plain English Handbook](https://www.sec.gov/pdf/handbook.pdf)).

Example:

| Less effective | Better |
|---|---|
| Except where prohibited by law or where retention is required for compliance, legal, security, or operational purposes, deletion requests may be submitted. | You may ask us to delete your information. We may keep information when the law allows or requires us to keep it, including for legal, security, compliance, or operational reasons. |

### Be transparent about limits

If there is a limitation, say it where it matters. Do not rely on a generic footer link to cure a claim, form, call to action, price, eligibility statement, health statement, or consent flow that needs a nearby disclosure.

FTC guidance says disclosures that are integral to a claim should appear on the same page and immediately next to the claim, especially for cost information and serious health or safety disclosures ([FTC Dot Com Disclosures](https://www.ftc.gov/sites/default/files/attachments/press-releases/ftc-staff-revises-online-advertising-disclosure-guidelines/130312dotcomdisclosures.pdf)).

Example:

| Risky | Better |
|---|---|
| Footer-only “Terms apply” link under a healthcare service claim. | Near the claim: “This service is not for emergencies. Call 911 or go to the nearest emergency room if you need emergency care.” |

### Treat rights and choices as usable

A right is not meaningfully communicated if the reader cannot find it, understand it, or exercise it. Privacy, access, deletion, correction, opt-out, complaint, cancellation, accessibility, language-assistance, and grievance rights should include practical next steps.

California privacy guidance expects privacy policies to describe consumer rights, methods for exercising rights, request forms or portals if offered, verification, opt-out preference signals, authorized agents, contact information, and the date last updated ([CPPA General Notices Guidance](https://cppa.ca.gov/pdf/general_notices.pdf)).

Example:

| Less effective | Better |
|---|---|
| Data subjects may exercise applicable rights by submitting a verifiable request. | You may have the right to ask us to access, delete, correct, or limit the use of certain personal information. To make a request, use the privacy email address or request form listed below. We may need to verify your identity before responding. |

### Avoid fear-based legal tone

Do not use legal language to intimidate readers into compliance. State consequences clearly, but do not exaggerate, moralize, or overstate. A calm sentence is often stronger than a threatening one.

Example:

| Less effective | Better |
|---|---|
| Any violation shall result in immediate termination and all remedies available at law and equity. | If you violate these terms, we may suspend or end your access. We may also use any legal remedies available to us. |

### Keep the organization accountable

A legal document should not make every user action sound mandatory while describing the organization’s commitments as vague and discretionary. If the organization has a legal or operational duty, say so.

Example:

| Less effective | Better |
|---|---|
| Requests will be handled as appropriate. | We will review your request and respond within the time required by law. |

## Legal verbs and modality

Use legal verbs consistently. Inconsistent modality creates ambiguity and weakens attorney review.

| Word | Use for | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Must | A requirement or obligation | You must provide accurate information. |
| Must not | A prohibition | You must not use the site for unlawful purposes. |
| Will | A commitment or future action | We will send notices to the email address you provide. |
| May | Permission, possibility, or discretion | We may update this policy from time to time. |
| Can | Ability or practical possibility | You can contact us by email. |
| Should | Recommendation, not a binding obligation | You should keep a copy of this policy for your records. |

Avoid “shall” in consumer-facing documents unless counsel requires it. Federal plain-language guidance commonly recommends “must” instead of “shall,” and California disclosure rules favor plain, straightforward language over legal jargon ([U.S. Office of Personnel Management Plain Language](https://www.opm.gov/information-management/plain-language/), [11 CCR § 7003](https://www.law.cornell.edu/regulations/california/11-CCR-7003)).

## Definitions

Definitions should help readers, not serve as drafting shortcuts. Define terms only when the definition improves precision, avoids genuine repetition, or maps to a legal requirement.

Use definitions sparingly in public-facing documents. The SEC warns that a defined term can become a shortcut for the writer and a roadblock for the reader ([SEC Plain English Handbook](https://www.sec.gov/pdf/handbook.pdf)).

Good definition:

> “Service” means the website, forms, tools, and support features we provide through this site.

Weak definition:

> “Platform” means any and all proprietary, licensed, hosted, enabled, embedded, derivative, associated, successor, or affiliated offerings made available by Company from time to time.

Do not capitalize ordinary words merely because they are defined. If a definition is essential, make the defined term intuitive and use it consistently.

## Structure

Organize legal documents around reader questions. A reader should be able to scan the headings and understand the document’s shape.

Preferred structure for public-facing legal documents:

1. What this document is
2. Who it applies to
3. What the organization does
4. What the reader can or must do
5. What information, rights, duties, risks, or limits matter
6. How choices, requests, complaints, cancellations, or opt-outs work
7. What happens when the document or service changes
8. How to contact the organization
9. Effective date or last updated date

Use headings that say what the section is about. Avoid vague headings like “Miscellaneous,” “General,” or “Other” unless the section truly contains residual administrative terms.

## Formatting and accessibility

Legal readability is partly visual. The SEC recommends descriptive headings, short explanatory sentences, bullet lists, tables for complex material, readable type, generous white space, left-justified ragged-right text, and avoidance of all-caps blocks because all-caps text is hard to read ([SEC Plain English Handbook](https://www.sec.gov/pdf/handbook.pdf)).

Use:

- Short sections with descriptive headings.
- Short paragraphs focused on one point.
- Bullets for lists of rights, steps, categories, examples, requirements, exceptions, and contact methods.
- Tables for complex comparisons, deadlines, categories, or choices.
- Real text rather than image-only text.
- Responsive web layouts.
- Specific link labels.
- Visible focus states and keyboard-accessible controls.
- Clear page language and section language for assistive technologies.

WCAG 2.2 organizes accessibility around content being perceivable, operable, understandable, and robust across devices and assistive technologies ([W3C WCAG 2.2](https://www.w3.org/TR/WCAG22/)). California regulations require online notices to be reasonably accessible to consumers with disabilities and reference WCAG 2.1 as a recognized standard for online notices ([11 CCR § 7003](https://www.law.cornell.edu/regulations/california/11-CCR-7003)).

Avoid:

- All-caps paragraphs.
- Dense blocks of legal text.
- Low-contrast links.
- Mouseover-only disclosures.
- PDF-only legal pages when the document is meant to function as a web disclosure.
- Generic “learn more” links for material disclosures.
- Footer-only placement for terms that should appear near a claim, form, button, price, or consent.

## Website footer documents

Footer links help users find full legal documents. They do not replace timely, contextual disclosures. A privacy policy in the footer does not by itself solve a missing notice at collection. A terms link in the footer does not by itself solve a missing price, eligibility, consent, health, or safety limitation near the relevant claim.

California guidance says a notice at collection must be provided at or before the point a business starts collecting personal information, and if personal information is collected online, the notice can be posted as a link on the webpage that collects the information ([CPPA General Notices Guidance](https://cppa.ca.gov/pdf/general_notices.pdf)). FTC guidance similarly emphasizes proximity, prominence, and understandable language for disclosures that qualify claims ([FTC Dot Com Disclosures](https://www.ftc.gov/sites/default/files/attachments/press-releases/ftc-staff-revises-online-advertising-disclosure-guidelines/130312dotcomdisclosures.pdf)).

Recommended footer labels:

| Document | Preferred footer label | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| General privacy policy | Privacy Policy | Include “Privacy” in the link label. |
| California privacy rights | California Privacy Notice | Use when California-specific terms are separate or prominent. |
| Website terms | Terms of Use | Use “Terms of Service” only if that is the organization’s convention. |
| HIPAA notice | Notice of Privacy Practices | Use when HIPAA applies and counsel confirms scope. |
| Accessibility | Accessibility Statement | Include assistance and feedback channels. |
| Nondiscrimination | Nondiscrimination Notice | Common in healthcare and civil-rights contexts. |
| Regulated marketing disclaimers | Important Disclaimers | Also place key disclaimers near relevant claims. |
| Health data practices | Health Data Privacy Notice | Use only after counsel confirms legal and product scope. |

Footer links should be visible, conventional, and accessible. California regulations state that required conspicuous website links must appear in a similar manner to other similarly posted homepage links, including approximate font size and color ([11 CCR § 7003](https://www.law.cornell.edu/regulations/california/11-CCR-7003)).

## Healthcare-first application

Healthcare documents require special care because readers may make decisions involving health, privacy, insurance, access, discrimination, emergencies, or sensitive personal information. The tone should be especially concrete, non-alarming, and action-oriented.

If HIPAA applies, the Notice of Privacy Practices must be in plain language and must describe how protected health information may be used and disclosed, individual rights and how to exercise them, legal duties, complaint options, contact information, and an effective date ([HHS HIPAA Notice Guidance](https://www.hhs.gov/hipaa/for-professionals/privacy/guidance/privacy-practices-for-protected-health-information/index.html)). HHS also provides model notices intended to give clear, user-friendly explanations of individual rights and privacy practices, with updates addressing 2026 Part 2-related notice requirements ([HHS Model Notices of Privacy Practices](https://www.hhs.gov/hipaa/for-professionals/privacy/guidance/model-notices-privacy-practices/index.html)).

Do not imply that HIPAA applies to every health-related website, form, app, or data flow. FTC guidance explains that many health apps, connected devices, and similar products are not covered by HIPAA but may be subject to the FTC Health Breach Notification Rule if they are vendors of personal health records, PHR-related entities, or third-party service providers ([FTC Health Breach Notification Rule Guidance](https://www.ftc.gov/business-guidance/resources/complying-ftcs-health-breach-notification-rule-0)).

Healthcare legal pages should be especially explicit about:

- Whether the site provides general information, administrative support, appointment help, insurance education, telehealth, clinical care, or something else.
- Whether the site is for emergencies.
- What to do in an emergency.
- Whether HIPAA applies.
- What privacy rights may exist.
- How to request records, privacy help, language assistance, disability accommodations, or complaint support.
- Whether marketing, insurance, eligibility, or provider-network limitations apply.

For healthcare nondiscrimination notices, use direct language about protected classes, free aids and services, free language services, how to request help, and how to file a grievance or complaint. HHS states that it does not discriminate based on race, color, national origin, age, disability, religion, or sex, and that it provides free aids and services for people with disabilities and free language services for people whose primary language is not English ([HHS Nondiscrimination Notice](https://www.hhs.gov/civil-rights/for-individuals/nondiscrimination/index.html)).

## California default overlay

When California may apply, legal and privacy documents should be drafted with California readability, accessibility, notice, and rights-exercise expectations in mind.

California regulations require consumer disclosures and communications to be easy to read and understandable, to use plain and straightforward language, to avoid technical or legal jargon, to be readable on smaller screens, to be available in the languages used for other California consumer-facing information, and to be reasonably accessible to consumers with disabilities ([11 CCR § 7003](https://www.law.cornell.edu/regulations/california/11-CCR-7003)).

For California privacy notices, include the operational details readers need. CPPA guidance says a notice at collection should include categories of personal information, including sensitive personal information, purposes of collection or use, whether information is sold or shared, intended retention length for each category, applicable opt-out links, and a link to the privacy policy ([CPPA General Notices Guidance](https://cppa.ca.gov/pdf/general_notices.pdf)).

California privacy policies should also explain rights and how to exercise them, including request methods, verification, opt-out preference signals, authorized agents, sensitive-personal-information use where applicable, contact information, and the date last updated ([CPPA General Notices Guidance](https://cppa.ca.gov/pdf/general_notices.pdf)).

## EU-compatible posture

Do not draft a US document as if GDPR applies unless counsel says it does. But do not draft in a way that makes future EU adaptation harder.

Preserve EU compatibility by writing privacy and rights language that is concise, transparent, intelligible, easily accessible, clear, and plain. That formulation aligns with GDPR Article 12’s standard for privacy information and rights communications ([GDPR Article 12](https://gdpr-info.eu/art-12-gdpr/)).

Avoid US-only assumptions when a neutral formulation works. For example, “privacy rights” may be more adaptable than “consumer rights” in a general section, while “California privacy rights” should be used when the California overlay specifically applies.

If EU coverage is later added, counsel can layer in controller or processor roles, lawful bases, international transfers, data-subject rights, supervisory-authority complaint rights, and EU-specific timing rules.

## Drafting workflow

### Build a source map first

Before drafting, identify the sources that control the document:

- Statutes and regulations.
- Agency guidance.
- Attorney instructions.
- Contract requirements.
- Product behavior.
- Data flows.
- Vendor roles.
- Support processes.
- Marketing claims.
- Required forms or model notices.
- Accessibility and language requirements.

The SEC recommends evaluating whether readers will understand the language, whether important information is missing, and whether information is included only because it has always been there ([SEC Plain English Handbook](https://www.sec.gov/pdf/handbook.pdf)).

### Draft from facts, not templates

Templates can provide structure, but they should not decide substance. Every statement must match the organization’s actual practices. If the organization does not know whether a statement is true, do not publish it until the owner confirms it.

For each section, ask:

- What legal requirement or business practice does this section express?
- Who owns the truth of this statement?
- What would make this statement inaccurate?
- What user decision depends on this statement?
- Does the section belong here, or closer to a claim, form, button, or consent?
- Has counsel approved this formulation?

### Use a layered model

Use layers to make complex documents readable without losing detail:

- **Overview**: A short, non-operative summary when useful.
- **Operative terms**: The legally meaningful rights, duties, permissions, restrictions, limits, and exceptions.
- **Practical instructions**: How to make requests, contact the organization, complain, cancel, opt out, or get help.
- **Jurisdictional modules**: California, HIPAA, sector-specific, or future EU sections where applicable.
- **Internal source register**: A non-public review file linking sections to sources, business owners, and attorney approval.

### Test the document

Readability scores are not enough. The SEC warns that readability formulas count syllables, words, and sentences but cannot determine whether a document actually conveys information clearly, and recommends audience-centered testing when possible ([SEC Plain English Handbook](https://www.sec.gov/pdf/handbook.pdf)). Federal plain-language guidance also emphasizes creating, designing, and testing content so the specific audience understands it ([Digital.gov Plain Language Guide](https://www.plainlanguage.gov/guidelines/)).

Use practical comprehension questions:

- What does this section let us do?
- What does this section require you to do?
- What choice do you have?
- What happens if you say no?
- How would you make a request or complaint?
- What information would you expect to find but cannot find?
- Which sentence sounds most confusing, evasive, or overly legal?

## Before-and-after examples

### General legal disclaimer

Less effective:

> The information contained herein is provided for informational purposes only and shall not be deemed to constitute legal, medical, financial, or other professional advice, nor shall any reliance thereon create any professional relationship.

Better:

> This site provides general information. It does not provide legal, medical, financial, or other professional advice. Using this site does not create a professional relationship with us unless we separately agree to that in writing.

### Health emergency disclaimer

Less effective:

> This website is not intended for emergent conditions or circumstances requiring immediate clinical intervention.

Better:

> This website is not for emergencies. If you have a medical emergency, call 911 or go to the nearest emergency room.

### Privacy notice at collection

Less effective:

> By submitting this form, user acknowledges collection and processing of personal data for legitimate business purposes and other purposes set forth in Company’s privacy disclosures.

Better:

> We collect the information you enter in this form, such as your name, contact details, and message. We use it to respond to you, operate this site, and meet legal and security requirements. Read our Privacy Policy to learn more.

### Terms update language

Less effective:

> Company reserves the right to revise, modify, amend, supplement, or otherwise update these Terms at any time in its sole and absolute discretion.

Better:

> We may update these Terms from time to time. When we make material changes, we will update the “Last updated” date and provide any notice required by law.

### Contact and complaint language

Less effective:

> Questions regarding these policies may be directed to the designated representative through channels made available by Company.

Better:

> If you have questions about this policy, contact us using the privacy contact listed below. If you believe your rights have been violated, you may also file a complaint as described below.

## Review checklist

### Legal review

- Has a licensed attorney confirmed the document’s purpose, required content, governing law, and jurisdictional assumptions?
- Has counsel confirmed whether federal law, California law, state privacy law, HIPAA, FTC rules, healthcare rules, insurance rules, civil-rights rules, or other sector rules apply?
- Has counsel confirmed whether EU or other non-US requirements are excluded, included, or reserved for a future module?
- Has counsel reviewed all liability limitations, warranty disclaimers, indemnities, arbitration provisions, class-action waivers, consents, privacy-rights language, health disclaimers, and complaint procedures?

### Factual review

- Does the document match actual product behavior, data collection, data sharing, retention, vendors, support channels, and operational practices?
- Are all links, forms, email addresses, mailing addresses, phone numbers, and portals accurate?
- Are all timelines and deadlines legally and operationally correct?
- Are all regulated claims verified by the appropriate compliance owner?

### Plain-language review

- Can an everyday reader understand the practical meaning?
- Are rights, duties, choices, limits, and consequences easy to find?
- Are legal terms defined only when needed?
- Are passive voice and vague boilerplate removed?
- Are long sentences split?
- Are complex lists converted to bullets or tables?
- Are examples used where they reduce ambiguity?

### Web and accessibility review

- Are material disclosures near the claims, buttons, forms, prices, eligibility statements, or consent choices they qualify?
- Are footer links visible, conventional, and accessible?
- Are required disclosures readable on mobile?
- Are links specific enough to tell the reader what they open?
- Can keyboard and screen-reader users reach, understand, and use the document?
- Is the document available in required languages and accessible formats?

### Maintenance review

- Does the document include an effective date or last updated date?
- Is there a named internal owner?
- Is there a source register?
- Are update triggers documented?
- Is attorney approval documented?
- Is there a review cadence?

## Final test

A legal document is on voice when it is legally careful, operationally true, easy to scan, easy to act on, and honest about the organization’s commitments and limits. The best legal tone is not more casual than the law allows and not more complex than the law requires.

The final test is whether a reader can answer four questions without hunting:

- What does this mean for me?
- What can I do?
- What can the organization do?
- How do I get help?

If the answer is no, the document needs another round of legal, content, design, or operational review.
