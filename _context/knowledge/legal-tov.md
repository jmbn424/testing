# Part I: Voice

## § 00: How to use this guide

This is the standard for the voice, language, and structure of every legal document we publish for a general audience. It exists so a writer, designer, or lawyer can make the same call we would, without guessing.

Our readers are a mix of everyday users and other professionals, and we write for both at once by writing plainly: clear text serves the non-expert and wastes no one's time. Deliberately dense "legalese" is the opposite of what we do. It wastes the reader's effort, invites errors to hide, and sends the wrong signal about who we are. Plain language here does not mean casual writing, simplified law, or less rigor. It is a precision discipline: it forces the drafter to understand a provision's legal effect well enough to state it directly.

**What this guide is**

- A reusable standard you apply to any contract, notice, or disclosure, in any industry.
- A set of defaults (United States, with California compliance built in, and healthcare-aware), plus optional layers you switch on for a specific industry or region (see § 17).
- A practical tool: rules, word lists, do/don't examples, and a checklist.

**What this guide is not**

- It is not legal advice and does not replace a supervising attorney's judgment.
- It does not override any law, regulator requirement, or mandated form wording. Where the law dictates exact text, the law wins (see the precision exception in § 21).
- It is not a brand or marketing voice guide. It governs legal and regulatory content specifically.

> **The order of operations**
> When two instructions seem to conflict, resolve them in this order: **(1)** the law and any mandated wording, **(2)** the supervising attorney's documented decision, **(3)** this guide, **(4)** personal preference. Most of the time none of these conflict, since clarity and compliance point the same way.

---

## § 01: Who reads this

Write every document for two readers at once.

- **The everyday user** needs to understand what the document means *for them*: their rights, their choices, what we can do, and how to get help. Treat this reader as the **readability floor**: if an ordinary person can't follow it, it isn't finished.
- **The legal professional** needs to confirm the document is complete, precise, internally consistent, and defensible. Treat this reader as the **precision standard**: never make them infer the intended legal effect from friendly but loose wording.

Don't make the everyday reader decode legal mechanics to understand a practical choice. Don't make the attorney guess at meaning that good drafting would have made explicit. Plain and precise are partners, not rivals.

Assume readers are busy, often on a phone, and task-oriented. Many won't read every word, and a term they never see can't bind or inform them. Assume some arrive under stress: a health worry, a financial decision, urgency, low trust, a disability, or limited English. The tone's job is to reduce confusion, never to add to it.

---

## § 02: The voice

Write as a **trusted, principled advocate** would: plain and precise, honest and measured, quietly authoritative. Credibility here is self-evident. It comes from being accurate, well-reasoned, and consistent, not from volume, ornament, or posturing. State positions plainly and stand behind them. Be the same on a routine notice as on a high-stakes one: composed, even-handed, firm where firmness is warranted, and never strident.

This is a warm, human voice, but never a chummy one. We respect the reader enough to be clear with them and direct enough to be trusted. Strength is understood without being demonstrated. We don't throw our weight around, and no one mistakes that for softness.

In practice, the voice sounds like a skilled attorney and a skilled communicator working together: careful enough to satisfy a court, clear enough for a layperson to act on.

---

## § 03: Our five principles

Every rule in this guide traces back to one of these. When in doubt, return to them.

1. **Clear over clever.** If the reader can't do the thing (understand a right, agree, opt out, cancel, or reach us), the document has failed, however elegant it reads. The reader should understand it the first time. Comprehension is the job.
2. **Plain, not dumbed down.** We write at a 6th–8th grade reading level (5th–6th for health and safety-critical content) without losing legal precision. When the law requires a defined term, we define it once, in plain words, and use it consistently.
3. **Respectful and direct.** We say "you" to the reader and "we" for ourselves, and we name who must do what, in the active voice. We don't lecture, threaten, moralize, or hide behind "information may be disclosed."
4. **Honest by design.** We surface what a reasonable person would want to know, before they'd want to know it. We never bury, manipulate, or use dark patterns. Leaving is as easy as joining.
5. **Accessible to everyone.** Our documents conform to WCAG 2.2 Level AA, work with assistive technology, come in the languages we market in, and are available in another format on request.

## § 04: This, never that

A quick calibration for the register we want. We aim for the left and steer away from the right.

| We are | …never |
|---|---|
| Authoritative | aggressive or threatening |
| Precise | pedantic or padded |
| Plain | vague or evasive |
| Warm and human | chummy or jokey |
| Confident | boastful or absolute |
| Calm | fear-based or punitive |
| Trustworthy & formal enough | stiff or archaic |

A note on calm: state consequences clearly, but don't use legal language to frighten people into compliance. "If you violate these terms, we may suspend or end your access" is stronger than a cascade of "shall result in immediate termination and all remedies available at law and equity."

## § 05: Tone by document type

Different documents sit at different points on the formality spectrum. Tone never changes the legal substance; it changes how clearly that substance lands. Use this to calibrate register.

| Document | Register | What the reader is asking |
|---|---|---|
| Privacy Policy | Informative, calm, direct | "What do you collect, and why?" |
| Terms of Service / Use | Firm, fair, clear | "What are the rules?" |
| Notice of Privacy Practices (HIPAA) | Clinical, plain, respectful | "What happens to my health data?" |
| Cookie Policy | Brief, light, direct | "What are you tracking?" |
| Disclaimer / Limitation of liability | Direct, unambiguous | "What are you responsible for?" |
| Arbitration / Dispute resolution | Neutral, fair, explanatory | "What happens if something goes wrong?" |
| Accessibility Statement | Inviting, committed | "Is this usable for me?" |
| Demand / enforcement letter | Resolute, measured | "How serious is this, and what now?" |

## § 06: Standards we follow

Our rules aren't preferences. They line up with recognized authority, which is what lets an attorney sign off with confidence and what we can point to if asked.

**Plain language**

- **ISO 24495-1:2023**, the international plain-language standard. Its test: readers can *find* what they need, *understand* what they find, and *use* it.
- **Plain Writing Act of 2010** and the **Federal Plain Language Guidelines** (plainlanguage.gov), the US public-sector benchmark.
- **SEC, *A Plain English Handbook***, a reminder that plain English means the *orderly, clear presentation* of complex information, not the deletion of it.
- **Bryan Garner** (*Legal Writing in Plain English* and *The Redbook*, 5th ed.) and **Joseph Kimble**, the leading modern legal-writing authorities.

**Reading-level targets**

| Content | Target | Measure with |
|---|---|---|
| General consumer text (terms, policies, disclaimers) | 6th–8th grade | Flesch–Kincaid Grade Level |
| Mixed consumer + professional documents | Grade 8 floor; precision intact | Flesch–Kincaid + editorial review |
| Health & safety-critical / consent content | 5th–6th grade | Flesch–Kincaid + CDC Clear Communication Index |
| Professional-only annexes (rare) | Plain, no fixed grade | Editorial review |

> **Read the score, don't worship it**
> Readability formulas count syllables and sentence length. They can't tell whether a document actually communicates. They also penalize numbered and bulleted clauses even when those make a document *easier* to use, and they're skewed by unavoidable proper nouns (HIPAA, ADMT). Test such text with and without those terms, treat the grade level as one signal among several, and pair it with a human read, ideally a non-lawyer who can summarize the obligations back to you. For health content, add a structured rubric like the CDC Index. A clear document that scores half a grade high beats a high-scoring one no one can follow.

# Part II: Language

## § 07: Words & grammar

### Use the active voice and name the actor

Say who does what. The passive voice hides responsibility and is the native tongue of evasive legal writing. Use it only when the actor is unknown, genuinely irrelevant, or deliberately secondary.

> **✗ Avoid:** `Personal information may be shared with third parties.`
>
> **✓ Use:** `We share your information with the partners listed in § 4.`

### Write to the reader

Use **"you"** for the reader and **"we / us / our"** for our organization. It is clearer, shorter, and warmer than "the User" and "the Company." Use the full legal entity name only where the distinction between related entities matters.

### Contractions are welcome

In consumer-facing documents, contractions ("we're," "don't," "you'll") make the writing sound human and read faster, and they don't affect enforceability. Use them naturally. Keep the fuller form where a sentence carries unusual legal weight or where it simply reads better. The goal is a steady, conversational rhythm, not forced informality.

### Choose the right obligation word

One of the oldest problems in legal drafting is that "shall" can mean must, will, may, or nothing at all. The federal court rules removed it. So do we. Pick the word that says exactly what you mean, and use it consistently. Inconsistent modality is what attorney review exists to catch.

| Use | For | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **must** | a duty / requirement | You **must** be 18 to open an account. |
| **must not** | a prohibition | You **must not** resell the service. |
| **will** | our promise or a future fact | We **will** respond within 30 days. |
| **may** | permission or discretion | You **may** cancel at any time. |
| **can** | ability / practical possibility | You **can** reach us by email. |
| **should** | a recommendation, not a duty | You **should** keep a copy for your records. |
| ~~shall~~ | — | Avoid. The only exception is verbatim statutory or mandated form text (log it, see § 21). |

### Keep sentences and paragraphs short

- Aim for an average sentence under **20 words**, one idea per sentence.
- Keep the subject, verb, and object together and near the front, so the reader doesn't have to hold a clause in memory to find out who does what.
- End sentences on the point that matters; put the important word last.
- Keep paragraphs to **five sentences or fewer**. Break a long obligation into a lead-in line plus a bulleted list.
- Prefer strong verbs over noun-strings (zombie nouns): ~~make a determination~~ → **decide**, ~~provide verification of~~ → **verify**, ~~remit payment~~ → **pay**.
- Use positive phrasing, and avoid stacked negatives: ~~notice will not be effective unless delivered~~ → **notice is effective only if delivered**. And ~~not uncommon~~ → **common**.

## § 08: Banished legalese

These words add nothing but distance. Every modern legal-writing authority agrees: cut them. The list below is banned by default; keep one only under the precision exception (§ 21).

**Archaic pointers** (~~herein, hereof, hereto, hereunder, hereinafter, heretofore, thereof, thereto, whereby, wherein~~): they're imprecise (which part do they point to?). Use **this Policy**, **this section**, **of it**, **under it**, **before now**.

**Recital flourishes** (~~WITNESSETH, WHEREAS, NOW THEREFORE, IN WITNESS WHEREOF, to wit~~): delete them. Replace a wall of "whereas" clauses with a short plain "Background" sentence.

**Fake-precise pointers** (~~said agreement, such party, the aforesaid, aforementioned~~): use **the**, **this**, **that**.

**Coupled synonyms** (~~null and void, indemnify and hold harmless, terms and conditions, any and all, each and every, force and effect, cease and desist~~): pick the one word that carries the meaning.

> **⚠ Two careful exceptions**
>
> **Terms of art.** A few phrases (for example *indemnify and hold harmless*, *best efforts*, *consequential damages*) have settled meaning from prior court decisions. Don't "plain-language" these away on your own. Flag them for the attorney, who may keep them under the precision exception.
>
> **Required wording.** Where a law mandates exact text (including a small set of disclosures that must appear in a particular form), keep it. Otherwise rewrite. (On all-caps specifically, see § 15.)

## § 10: Defined terms

- **Define only when it earns its place.** A defined term should save real repetition or remove real ambiguity. Don't define a word you use twice. A definition is a shortcut for the writer and can be a roadblock for the reader. Use them sparingly in public-facing documents.
- **Define in plain words, once,** on first use, either inline (in parentheses) or in a short glossary near the top for longer documents. If a document needs many definitions, move them to a schedule at the end so they don't block the reader from the operative terms.
- **Then use the term consistently.** Never switch between "Services," "Platform," and "site" for the same thing.
- **Keep defined terms intuitive,** and don't capitalize ordinary words just because they're defined.
- **Don't smuggle obligations into a definition.** Definitions say what a word means. They don't make rules.

---

## § 11: Numbers, dates, money

- **Numerals for anything operative** (amounts, days, ages, percentages): "within 30 days," "you must be 18." They're easier to scan and harder to misread.
- **Drop the "words (numerals)" doubling.** Write **30 days**, not ~~thirty (30) days~~, unless a mandated form requires both.
- **Write dates unambiguously:** **June 7, 2026** (or ISO **2026-06-07**), never ~~6/7/26~~, which means different things in different countries.
- **Currency with the code where audiences are mixed:** "USD 49" or "$49 (USD)."
- **Express risk and frequency in plain terms** the reader can picture, and avoid bare statistics in health content without a concrete frame.

---

## § 12: Inclusive language

We frame this as a matter of **clarity and respect**, which is universally defensible: precise, person-respecting language is simply better legal writing.

- Use singular **"they"** for an unknown or unspecified person. Avoid "he/she" and "s/he."
- Prefer the plural to sidestep the issue entirely: "**users manage their accounts**" beats "~~the user manages his account~~."
- Drop gendered defaults: ~~chairman, manpower~~ → **chair, staff / workforce**.
- Don't assume race, ability, family structure, or marital status where it isn't relevant.
- For groups, use the term the community uses, and follow current usage on capitalizing identity terms.

# Part III: Structure & design

## § 13: Layered structure

People skim. Give them a way in. Every footer document uses a **layered** shape: a short plain-language summary up top, then the full, legally complete text below. Lead with the practical point: state the rule before the exception, the action before the process, the consequence before the citation. The summary informs. The full text governs. We say so.

**The document skeleton**

1. **Title**, what it is, in the words people use ("Privacy Policy," not "Data Processing Instrument").
2. **Effective date** and **Last updated** date, shown at the top.
3. **Plain-language summary**, 3–5 sentences or a short bullet list (the "layer one" notice).
4. **Table of contents** with in-page anchor links.
5. **Numbered sections** with descriptive headings ("3. What we collect," not "3. Information").
6. **Defined terms**, inline on first use, or a short glossary / end schedule for longer documents.
7. **Your rights and choices, and how to use them**, with every channel the law requires, and concrete next steps.
8. **How to contact us:** name, email, mailing address, and any required phone or complaint channel.
9. **Change log:** date plus a one-line description of each material change.
10. **Print / download** option and a language switcher where relevant.

> **Summaries help, but the full text controls**
> A summary is a courtesy, not a loophole. Make sure it's accurate and add a short line such as: "This summary is here to help. The full terms below are what apply." Never let the summary contradict or soften what the full text actually does.

A right the reader can't find, understand, or act on hasn't really been communicated. State each right plainly and tell the reader exactly how to exercise it.

---

## § 14: Formatting

- **Headings:** sentence case, descriptive, nested no deeper than three levels in body content. Avoid "Miscellaneous," "General," or "Other" unless the section is truly residual.
- **Numbering:** tiered decimal (1, 1.1, 1.1.1). Avoid the Roman→letter→numeral cascade that no one can cite.
- **Lists:** use them for anything with three or more parallel items. Bullets for sets, numbers for sequence or things you'll cross-reference.
- **Tables:** for complex comparisons, deadlines, categories, or choices, with a caption and real column headers so screen readers can navigate them.
- **Cross-references** by section number ("see § 4"), never "as set forth herein."
- **Body text:** at least 16px on the web, 12-point minimum in print/PDF.
- **Links:** describe the destination ("read our Cookie Policy"), never "click here" or a bare "learn more."
- **White space:** use it generously between clauses. An approachable page invites reading, a wall of text repels it.
- **All-caps blocks:** only where a law requires them (see § 15).

---

## § 15: Conspicuous without shouting

Lawyers long used ALL-CAPS to make warranty disclaimers and liability limits "conspicuous." We don't, except where a law genuinely requires it.

The empirical case is settled and it cuts against caps. A study of standard form contracts from 500 large consumer companies found all-caps does **not** improve the quality of consent: readers recall and understand capitalized terms no better than ordinary text, and *worse* in older readers. In one test of a free-trial cancellation policy, a clear majority of respondents over 55 answered comprehension questions wrong when the clause was in all-caps, versus far fewer in the sentence-case version. All-caps reads as "textual yelling," slows everyone down, and, because most consumer contracts contain at least one all-caps block, has trained readers to skip past those blocks entirely (the "habituation trap").

So "conspicuous" is a *performance* standard, not a font trick. Regulators ask whether an ordinary person actually notices and understands a disclosure. Achieve that through hierarchy rather than capitals:

| Tool | How to use it | Watch out for |
|---|---|---|
| **Bold** | Highlight a key term, deadline, or sentence | If everything is bold, nothing is, so use it sparingly |
| **Callout box / border** | Set off a critical limitation, opt-out, or warning | Don't overuse, or it becomes wallpaper |
| **Sentence case** | The default for all body text, however important | — |
| **Bullets** | Break enumerated rights or conditions out of a long sentence | Keep items parallel |
| **White space** | Separate and frame important clauses | — |

Keep all-caps only where a statute requires conspicuous text in capitals, and log it as a precision exception (§ 21).

## § 16: Honesty & non-deception

Tone is more than wording. It's whether the design is fair. Regulators judge a disclosure by whether an ordinary person actually notices and understands it (the "clear and conspicuous" test), and they treat manipulative design ("dark patterns") as deceptive on its own. A disclosure that's integral to a claim belongs next to that claim, not parked in a remote policy.

**What this means for us**

- **Conspicuous, not buried.** Material terms sit where the reader will see them, near the choice they affect (by the price, the form, the button, or the consent), not hidden in a footnote or behind a scroll.
- **Real consent.** No pre-checked boxes, no consent bundled with unrelated terms, no "I agree" that quietly covers more than it says.
- **Symmetry.** Cancelling, unsubscribing, or opting out is at least as easy as signing up was: same number of steps, same prominence.
- **No false urgency or shame.** No countdowns, guilt-worded decline buttons, or visual tricks that steer the choice.
- **Plain choices.** Options are labeled in plain words so the reader knows what each one does.
- **Accountable, not lopsided.** Don't make every user duty sound mandatory while our own commitments stay vague. If we have a legal or operational duty, say so: "We will respond within the time the law requires."

> **Why this lives in a tone guide**
> Because the same instinct that produces honest wording produces honest design. If a sentence only works when the reader misses it, rewrite the sentence rather than hide it.

# Part IV: Applying it

## § 17: Modular layers

The guide is built in layers so it stays reusable. Start with the universal layer, then switch on whichever others apply. At the top of each document, note which layers are in force.

**Universal: US baseline** · *Always on*
Everything in this guide: plain language, the five principles, banished legalese, layered structure, accessibility, and honest design. This is the floor for every document, every industry.

**California add-on** · *Default on*
California privacy law requires "plain, straightforward language," free of technical or legal jargon, readable on small screens, available in the languages we use for other consumer content, and reasonably accessible to people with disabilities. So our defaults already align. Layer on its specific notices (notice at collection, the "Do Not Sell or Share" choice, the annual update with a 12-month look-back, retention by category) and watch newer obligations such as plain-language pre-use notices for automated decision-making.

**Healthcare add-on** · *Healthcare-aware by default, full layer on first healthcare use*
HIPAA requires a Notice of Privacy Practices in plain language, historically the most-failed requirement, which is exactly where this guide helps. Tighten the reading level to 5th–6th grade, check health content against the CDC Clear Communication Index, and don't imply HIPAA covers every health-related site or app (many are governed instead by the FTC's authority and Health Breach Notification Rule). For the full website layer, see the companion **Healthcare Website Specific** document.

**EU-compatible posture** · *Future*
Not a default, and we don't draft as if GDPR applies unless counsel says it does. But we don't make future adaptation harder, either. Writing privacy and rights language that is "concise, transparent, intelligible, and easily accessible, using clear and plain language," and keeping consumer text at roughly a B2 (upper-intermediate) level, already aligns with EU transparency expectations and the European Accessibility Act. Prefer neutral phrasing ("privacy rights") in general sections and reserve "California privacy rights" for the California overlay. When we expand, counsel layers in controller/processor roles, lawful bases, transfers, and EU-specific rights.

> **A note on currency**
> Specific rules, thresholds, deadlines, and enforcement figures change, and several in the source research are snapshots. This guide names the standards and the *shape* of the obligations. It is not the live text of any statute. Before publishing, confirm the current requirement with the supervising attorney. See the watch-list in § 21.

## § 18: Worked examples

The same content, before and after. Nothing legally meaningful is lost, only the fog.

**A termination clause**

> **Before** · 58 words, ~grade 17
> *"In the event that the User shall wish to terminate the Account, the User must provide written notice to the Company prior to the expiration of the then-current billing period, whereupon the Company shall, notwithstanding the foregoing, retain such data as may be required pursuant to applicable law."*

> **After** · 36 words, ~grade 8
> *"You can close your account at any time. To avoid being charged for the next period, tell us before the current one ends. After you close it, we keep only the data the law requires us to keep."*

**A limitation of liability**

> **Before** *"Notwithstanding the foregoing, in no event shall Company be liable for any indirect, incidental, special, exemplary, consequential, or punitive damages, howsoever caused and regardless of the theory of liability…"*

> **After** *"To the extent the law allows, our liability is limited to the amount you paid us in the 12 months before the claim arose."*

**A data-use statement**

> **Before** *"Personal information may be disclosed to third-party service providers for business purposes including but not limited to the processing and administration of claims."*

> **After** *"We share your name and appointment date with your insurance provider to process your claim."*

**A terms-update line**

> **Before** *"Company reserves the right to revise, modify, amend, supplement, or otherwise update these Terms at any time in its sole and absolute discretion."*

> **After** *"We may update these Terms from time to time. When we make material changes, we'll update the 'Last updated' date and give any notice the law requires."*

What changed across all four: active voice, "you" and "we," "shall" and "notwithstanding the foregoing" gone, one idea per sentence, the consequence stated plainly. What stayed: the timing rule, the legal-retention carve-out, the liability cap, the discretion to update, every operative term.

## § 19: Drafting workflow

**1. Build a source map first.** Before drafting, list what controls the document: statutes and regulations, agency guidance, attorney instructions, contract requirements, actual product behavior, data flows, vendor roles, support processes, marketing claims, and any required forms. You can't write a clause plainly until you know its real legal effect.

**2. Draft from facts, not templates.** Templates give structure, not substance. Every statement must match what the organization actually does. For each section ask: what requirement or practice does this express, who owns the truth of it, what would make it false, and what reader decision depends on it? If no one can confirm a statement is true, don't publish it.

**3. Work in layers.** Include a plain-language overview where it helps, the operative terms, practical instructions for requests, contact, complaints, and opt-outs, the jurisdictional modules in § 17, and a non-public source register that links each section to its source, owner, and attorney approval.

**4. Test it.** Readability scores aren't enough. They can't tell whether meaning came through. Have a non-lawyer read it and answer, without hunting: what does this let us do, what does it require of you, what choice do you have, what happens if you say no, and how would you make a request? The sentence they find most confusing or evasive is the one to rewrite.

## § 20: Pre-publish checklist

Run this before any legal document goes live.

- [ ] **Reading level on target.** Flesch–Kincaid within range for the content type. Health content also checked against a structured rubric.
- [ ] **"Shall" eliminated.** Obligations use must / must not / will / may / can / should, consistently. Any surviving "shall" is mandated text and is logged.
- [ ] **Banished legalese removed** (herein, whereas, pursuant to, doublets). Surviving terms of art are flagged and approved.
- [ ] **Active voice; reader addressed as "you"; actor named.**
- [ ] **Layered structure present:** summary, dates, TOC, descriptive headings, rights + how to use them, contact, change log.
- [ ] **Accessibility verified to WCAG 2.2 AA:** semantic headings, 4.5:1 contrast, no color-only meaning, keyboard reachable, real text version, descriptive links.
- [ ] **No all-caps blocks** except where a law requires them (logged).
- [ ] **Honest design:** material terms conspicuous and near the choice they affect, real consent, leaving as easy as joining, no dark patterns.
- [ ] **Disclosures placed contextually,** not relegated to the footer when they belong by a claim, form, price, or button.
- [ ] **Statements match actual practice,** with all links, addresses, phone numbers, portals, and deadlines verified.
- [ ] **Applicable layers applied** (California by default, healthcare and region as needed) and current requirements confirmed with counsel.
- [ ] **Mandated elements present** for the document type (required notices and contact channels).
- [ ] **Supervising attorney has reviewed and signed off,** with sign-off recorded in the change log.

## § 21: Governance

### Ownership & review

- **Co-owned** by Legal and Content. Legal owns what must be said. Content owns how it's said.
- **Reviewed annually**, and within 60 days of any relevant regulatory change. (Privacy policies generally need a refresh at least every 12 months, and a lapse has itself drawn enforcement.)
- **Every published document and this guide** carry a version number, an effective date, a last-updated date, a named internal owner, and a change log.

### The legal-precision exception

> **⚠ How we keep a term this guide would otherwise cut**
> A supervising attorney may keep a defined term of art, a mandated "shall," required all-caps, or other non-plain wording **when the law or settled legal meaning calls for it**. The condition: record the exception in the change log with a one-line reason. This keeps the guide honest and the decision deliberate, not an accident of habit. Garner's own view is that this exception should be narrow.

### Regulatory watch-list

Re-check the relevant module promptly if any of these move:

- The FTC issues new subscription / negative-option or disclosure rules, or new dark-patterns guidance.
- The DOJ issues or revises a web-accessibility rule for private businesses (WCAG 2.1 AA is the current de facto standard).
- California's privacy regulator amends its regulations, adjusts thresholds, or issues new enforcement guidance, including on automated decision-making.
- HHS updates the HIPAA model notices or their content/deadlines.
- We enter a new industry or region, which means building or activating the matching layer.

### What to measure

- Reading-level score per document.
- Accessibility audit pass rate.
- Volume and median turnaround of consumer-rights requests.
- Any regulator inquiries or complaints about clarity.

# Part V: Reference

## § 22: The final test

A legal document is on voice when it is legally careful, operationally true, easy to scan, easy to act on, and honest about both our commitments and our limits: no more casual than the law allows, and no more complex than the law requires.

The final test is whether a reader can answer four questions without hunting:

1. **What does this mean for me?**
2. **What can I do?**
3. **What can the organization do?**
4. **How do I get help?**

If the answer to any is no, the document needs another round of legal, content, design, or operational review.

## § 23: Glossary

| Term | What we mean |
|---|---|
| **Plain language** | Writing whose words, structure, and design let the intended reader find, understand, and use the information, per the ISO 24495-1 standard. |
| **Layered notice** | A short plain-language summary on top of the full, controlling legal text. |
| **Clear and conspicuous** | A disclosure an ordinary person would actually notice and understand, the regulator's performance test for a fair disclosure. |
| **Dark pattern** | A design that steers or tricks a reader into a choice they didn't mean to make. Banned. |
| **Term of art** | A word or phrase with a settled legal meaning from prior court decisions, which may be kept under the precision exception. |
| **WCAG 2.2 AA** | The web-accessibility conformance level we meet across our documents. |
| **Notice at collection** | The disclosure given at or before the point personal information is collected. |
| **Precision exception** | The logged, attorney-approved decision to keep non-plain wording the law requires (§ 21). |

## § 24: Authorities

The standards behind the rules, for the attorney's confidence and for anyone who asks why we write this way. Confirm current text and effective dates from the official source before relying on any specific requirement.

- **Plain language:** ISO 24495-1:2023; Plain Writing Act of 2010 and the Federal Plain Language Guidelines (plainlanguage.gov); SEC, *A Plain English Handbook*; Bryan A. Garner, *Legal Writing in Plain English* and *The Redbook* (5th ed.); Joseph Kimble, *Lifting the Fog of Legalese*.
- **Health literacy:** CDC Clear Communication Index; AHRQ Health Literacy Universal Precautions Toolkit; AMA / NIH reading-level guidance.
- **Honesty & disclosure:** FTC Act § 5 and the "clear and conspicuous" standard (FTC *Dot Com Disclosures*); FTC guidance on dark patterns and negative-option marketing; FTC Health Breach Notification Rule.
- **All-caps evidence:** Arbel & Toler, empirical study of all-caps in consumer contracts (capitalization does not improve, and can worsen, comprehension).
- **California:** California Consumer Privacy Act and its implementing regulations, including the "plain, straightforward language" requirement (11 CCR § 7003) and notice guidance from the California Privacy Protection Agency.
- **Healthcare:** HIPAA Notice of Privacy Practices rule (45 CFR § 164.520), which mandates plain language; current HHS model notices.
- **Accessibility:** WCAG 2.2 Level AA (our conformance level; 2.1 AA remains the common regulatory floor and is fully covered by 2.2); DOJ ADA web-accessibility rule for public entities.
- **EU-compatible posture:** GDPR Article 12 (clear and plain language); European Accessibility Act.
